# American Shad (Alosa sapidissima)

Also known as: shad, Atlantic shad, white shad; roe shad (female), buck shad (male).

## What is an American shad?

The American shad is the largest member of the herring family in North America, commonly reaching 4 to 8 pounds. It is anadromous — born in fresh water, living most of its life in the ocean, and returning to its natal river to spawn. Shad range along the Atlantic coast and historically ran up nearly every river on the seaboard. They are silvery and deep-bodied with a row of dark spots behind the gill cover, and they fill an important role in the food web as both predator and prey. Shad are prized by anglers for their hard fight on light tackle during the spring spawning runs.

## What does an American shad eat?

In the ocean, adult shad feed mainly on plankton — copepods and other zooplankton — along with small crustaceans (like mysids) and occasionally small fish. Young shad (fry) in the river feed on freshwater plankton and aquatic insects before migrating to sea. During the spawning run, adults eat little but will strike small darts and flashy lures, which is what makes them a fun fly- and light-tackle target.

## When is an American shad in season?

Shad are best known for their spring spawning runs, when adults ascend coastal rivers — the timing moves later as you go north along the coast. Seasons and limits (and in some rivers, full closures to protect depleted runs) are set by state agencies and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an American shad spawn?

American shad return from the ocean to their birth river in spring to spawn in fresh water. After spawning, fry hatch and grow through the summer feeding on plankton and insects, then cooling water in late summer and early fall triggers a mass downstream migration to the ocean, where the young grow to maturity over several years before returning to spawn.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both. American shad are anadromous: they spawn and spend their first months in fresh water, then live most of their adult lives in the ocean, returning to fresh water to spawn.

## Fun fact / angler note

The species name *sapidissima* means "most delicious" — shad have long been valued as food, and shad roe is a celebrated springtime delicacy. The spring shad run is a classic East Coast angling tradition, and shad's willingness to hit small bright darts makes them a light-tackle and fly-rod favorite.

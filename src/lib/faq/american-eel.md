# American Eel (Anguilla rostrata)

Also known as: eel, common eel, silver eel (mature stage), glass eel / elver (young stages).

## What is an American eel?

The American eel is a long, slender, snake-like fish covered in a slick mucus layer, the only freshwater eel native to North America. It is catadromous — the opposite of salmon — meaning it lives most of its life in fresh and brackish water but migrates to the ocean to spawn. American eels range along the Atlantic coast from Greenland and Canada south to South America, and inland through coastal rivers, lakes, and estuaries (including waters like Lake Champlain). Females grow much larger than males, reaching up to about 4 feet. Eels pass through several dramatically different life stages with different names.

## What does an American eel eat?

American eels are nocturnal carnivores. They hunt mostly at night, feeding on crustaceans, aquatic insects, worms, mollusks, small fish, and fish eggs, and will also scavenge. During the day they hide among snags, plants, rocks, and burrows close to shelter.

## When is an American eel in season?

Eels are present year-round in coastal rivers and estuaries where they live as adults, becoming more active in warmer months and at night. The eel fishery (including the valuable glass-eel/elver stage in some states) is tightly regulated, with seasons and limits set by state agencies and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission, and these change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping eels.

## When does an American eel spawn?

American eels have one of the most remarkable life cycles in the fish world. All American eels spawn in the Sargasso Sea — a region of the open Atlantic east of the Bahamas and south of Bermuda — generally in winter and spring. After hatching, the leaf-shaped larvae (leptocephali) drift on ocean currents toward the coast, transforming into transparent "glass eels," then pigmented "elvers" that migrate up rivers. They grow for years (sometimes decades) in fresh and brackish water as "yellow eels," then transform into "silver eels" and make the long migration back to the Sargasso Sea to spawn once and die.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both. The American eel is catadromous: it spawns in the open ocean (the Sargasso Sea) but spends most of its life growing in fresh and brackish water — rivers, lakes, and estuaries. This is why it appears in both freshwater and saltwater settings.

## Fun fact / angler note

Every American eel on Earth is born in the same place — the Sargasso Sea — and no one has ever directly observed them spawning there, despite more than a century of searching. An eel caught in a Vermont lake and one caught in a Florida river began life in the very same patch of ocean.

# Atlantic Mackerel (Scomber scombrus)

Also known as: mackerel, Boston mackerel, common mackerel.

## What is an Atlantic mackerel?

The Atlantic mackerel is a sleek, fast-swimming, schooling pelagic fish recognizable by its iridescent blue-green back marked with dark wavy bars and a silvery belly. It is highly migratory, ranging in the western Atlantic from Labrador to North Carolina, and travels in large schools in open water rather than along the bottom. Mackerel are an important forage fish, prey for many larger predators, and a popular light-tackle catch when schools move inshore.

## What does an Atlantic mackerel eat?

Atlantic mackerel are active plankton and small-prey feeders. They eat copepods and other zooplankton, small crustaceans, fish eggs and larvae, and small fish such as sand lance. They feed actively in the water column as they move with their schools, filtering and chasing small prey.

## When is an Atlantic mackerel in season?

Mackerel are a cool-season migratory presence in the Northeast, often appearing inshore in spring (and again in fall in places) as schools migrate along the coast. Seasons and limits are set by NOAA Fisheries and state agencies and can change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an Atlantic mackerel spawn?

Atlantic mackerel are broadcast spawners that release buoyant, pelagic eggs into open water. In the northwest Atlantic there are two spawning components: a southern group that spawns along the U.S. East Coast in roughly March–April, and a northern group that spawns around the Gulf of St. Lawrence in roughly June–July. Eggs and larvae drift in the water column, and the young join schools as they grow.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Atlantic mackerel are a pelagic marine species living in open coastal and offshore waters, not on the bottom and not in fresh water.

## Fun fact / angler note

When a mackerel school moves through, the action can be fast and furious — anglers often catch them several at a time on multi-hook "mackerel trees" (sabiki rigs). They're also a top live or cut bait for larger gamefish like striped bass, bluefish, and tuna.

# Reading Tide Charts

AnglerCast pulls tide predictions from NOAA's Center for Operational Oceanographic Products and Services (CO-OPS) for every saltwater destination, so you can plan your trip around the tide cycle. To use that information well, it helps to understand what the numbers actually mean.

## Tide tables vs. tide charts

Technically, what most people call a "tide chart" is a tide table — a list of times and heights of upcoming high and low tides at a specific tide station. A true tide chart is something different: a map showing water levels across a whole bay at a single moment. AnglerCast displays tide-table-style data — when the next high tide is, when the next low tide is, and how high or low the water will be at each. NOAA's tide tables cover more than 3,000 U.S. coastal locations.

## How many tides per day

Most U.S. coastal areas have two high tides and two low tides every day, separated by roughly 6 hours and 12 minutes. Some places — the Gulf Coast is the most common example — only get one high and one low tide per day. The two highs (or two lows) in the same day are not always the same height. That is normal; it is just how the gravitational pull of the sun and moon stacks up in your area.

## What the heights mean

A tide height is measured in feet above (or below) a reference level called Mean Lower Low Water, or MLLW. MLLW is the average of the lowest daily tide observed at that station over a 19-year period. If the tide table shows the next high tide as 6.4 ft, the water will be 6.4 feet above that long-term average low. If it shows -0.5 ft, the water will be half a foot below the average low — what anglers call a "negative tide." Negative tides expose more structure and can be great times to scout flats, oyster bars, or reefs you do not normally see.

## Tides do not move at a constant rate

This is the most important thing to understand for fishing. The water does not rise from low to high at a steady speed. It starts slowly, accelerates through the middle of the cycle, and slows down again as it approaches the next high or low. Most of the actual water movement — the current that pushes baitfish and triggers feeding — happens during the middle hours of the tide change.

Boaters use a rough guide called the Rule of Twelfths: about one twelfth of the total tide range moves in the first hour, two twelfths in the second, three twelfths in the third, three again in the fourth, two in the fifth, and one in the sixth. For fishing, this means the strongest current — and often the best bite — happens in the second, third, and fourth hours of an incoming or outgoing tide. The hours right around peak high and peak low are the slowest.

## Using tide data in AnglerCast

When you enter a saltwater destination on the Explore page, AnglerCast queries the nearest NOAA tide station and shows you the upcoming high and low tide times and heights. Use that information to time your trip around moving water. If your target tide change happens at 3:14 p.m., plan to be on the water by mid-morning so you are fishing through the productive middle hours of the change, not just arriving in time for slack.

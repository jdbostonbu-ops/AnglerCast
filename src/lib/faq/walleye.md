# Walleye (Sander vitreus)

Also known as: walleye, walleyed pike, yellow pickerel, marble eye, 'eye.

## What is a walleye?

The walleye is a prized cool-water gamefish and the largest member of the perch family, named for its large, glassy, light-reflecting eyes. Those eyes contain a reflective layer (the tapetum lucidum) that lets walleye see well in low light and murky water, so they feed most actively at dawn, dusk, night, and on overcast or windy days. Walleye are olive-gold with a white belly, sharp teeth, and spiny fins, living in large lakes, reservoirs, and rivers. They're highly sought after for their hard fight and outstanding, mild-flavored fillets.

## What does a walleye eat?

Walleye are efficient fish-eating predators. Yellow perch are a primary prey in many waters, along with other small fish such as minnows, shiners, ciscoes, and shad; younger walleye also eat insects and invertebrates. Their light-sensitive eyes give them a hunting edge in dim conditions, so they often feed when other predators can't see as well.

## When is a walleye in season?

Walleye are caught year-round, with strong fishing in spring around the spawn, in low-light summer periods, and through the ice in winter. They are managed with seasons, size limits, and bag limits (including spring closures in some waters to protect spawning fish) set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a walleye spawn?

Walleye spawn in early spring, shortly after ice-out, when water reaches roughly 45–50°F. They move into rivers, stream mouths, and rocky shoals and broadcast their eggs over gravel, rubble, and rocky bottom — there is no nest and no parental care. The eggs settle into the rocks to incubate. Walleye runs up tributary rivers in spring are a classic event, and many fisheries are supported partly by stocking.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Walleye live in freshwater lakes, reservoirs, and rivers.

## Fun fact / angler note

A walleye's glowing eyes are the secret to its success — the reflective layer behind the retina (the same thing that makes a deer's eyes shine in headlights) lets it hunt in dim, stained water where prey can't see it coming. That's why the best walleye fishing is often at dawn, dusk, after dark, or on a "walleye chop" of wind and clouds. They're also widely considered one of the best-eating freshwater fish.

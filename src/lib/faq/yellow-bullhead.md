# Yellow Bullhead (Ameiurus natalis)

Also known as: yellow bullhead catfish, bullhead, horned pout, butter cat, creek cat.

## What is a yellow bullhead?

The yellow bullhead is a small, hardy catfish closely related to the brown bullhead, common in ponds, lakes, and slow streams across the eastern U.S. and beyond. It is scaleless and smooth-skinned with a squared-off (not forked) tail, a yellowish-olive to brown body, and — its key field mark — pale yellowish or whitish chin barbels (the brown bullhead's are dark). Yellow bullheads tolerate warm, weedy, low-oxygen, even somewhat polluted water, and they're often the most commonly caught catfish in many small waters. They feed mostly at night and are a willing, easy catch.

## What does a yellow bullhead eat?

Yellow bullheads are bottom-feeding omnivores and scavengers. They eat aquatic insects and larvae, crayfish, snails, worms, small fish, and plant material, finding food by smell and taste with their sensitive barbels. They're active feeders after dark and will take a wide range of natural and scented baits.

## When is a yellow bullhead in season?

Yellow bullheads are caught spring through fall, especially on warm evenings. They're usually managed under general or catfish/panfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a yellow bullhead spawn?

Yellow bullheads spawn in late spring and early summer, fanning out a nest in shallow water near cover. As with other bullheads, the parents guard the eggs and then the school of young, herding the tight cluster of black fry through the shallows for a time after hatching.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Yellow bullheads live in warm freshwater ponds, lakes, and slow streams, tolerating weedy, low-oxygen conditions.

## Fun fact / angler note

The quickest way to identify a yellow bullhead is to check the chin whiskers — they're pale yellow or whitish, while the look-alike brown bullhead has dark ones. Yellow bullheads are famously tolerant and willing biters, which makes them a dependable catch in small ponds and creeks. Mind the sharp pectoral and dorsal spines when handling — that old "horned pout" name is a reminder they can jab.

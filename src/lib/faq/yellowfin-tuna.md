# Yellowfin Tuna (Thunnus albacares)

Also known as: yellowfin, ahi, allison tuna.

## What is a yellowfin tuna?

The yellowfin tuna is a sleek, powerful, warm-water pelagic predator named for its bright yellow fins and the long, sickle-shaped second dorsal and anal fins of larger fish. It is metallic dark blue above, yellow along the sides, and silvery below. Yellowfin are highly migratory and found in warm offshore waters worldwide, ranging into the Northeast canyons and offshore grounds in the warmer months. They are fast, hard-fighting, and a top offshore sportfish, as well as a major commercial and sushi-market species (sold as "ahi").

## What does a yellowfin tuna eat?

Yellowfin are fast visual predators that feed throughout the water column on small fish (such as flying fish, sauries, and small mackerels), squid, and crustaceans. They often hunt in schools, sometimes associating with dolphins and floating objects, and chase down prey with high-speed bursts.

## When is a yellowfin tuna in season?

Yellowfin appear off the Northeast seasonally, mainly in the warmer months on offshore canyon and bank grounds when warm water and bait move in. They are managed federally by NOAA Fisheries and internationally by ICCAT, with permits and limits that change from year to year. Always check current federal regulations and permit requirements before targeting them.

## When does a yellowfin tuna spawn?

Yellowfin are batch spawners that release pelagic eggs into warm open-ocean waters, spawning year-round in tropical waters and seasonally toward the edges of their range. Eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift in warm water, and the young grow quickly. They reach maturity relatively young compared to bluefin.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Yellowfin tuna are a highly migratory open-ocean (pelagic) marine species of warm offshore waters.

## Fun fact / angler note

Yellowfin are prized for their speed and stamina — hooking one offshore means a hard, dogged fight, often straight down. On the table and at market they're "ahi," a staple of sashimi and seared-tuna dishes. They're frequently found around floating debris, weed lines, and (in some regions) in mixed schools with dolphins.

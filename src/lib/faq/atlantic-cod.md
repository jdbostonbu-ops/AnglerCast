# Atlantic Cod (Gadus morhua)

Also known as: cod, codfish, scrod (small cod).

## What is an Atlantic cod?

The Atlantic cod is a large, historically vital groundfish of the North Atlantic, ranging in the western Atlantic from Greenland and Newfoundland south to Cape Hatteras, with highest densities in the north (Newfoundland, the Gulf of Maine, and Georges Bank). It has a heavy body, three rounded dorsal fins, a distinct chin barbel, and a pale lateral line. Cod live near the bottom on a range of habitats and have supported some of the most famous fisheries in history. They can grow large and live for years, though heavy historical fishing has reduced many stocks.

## What does an Atlantic cod eat?

Atlantic cod are opportunistic bottom predators whose diet shifts with size. Larvae eat tiny copepods; juveniles eat small crustaceans; and adults become strongly fish-eating, taking herring, mackerel, sand lance, smaller cod, and other fish, along with crabs, shrimp, mollusks, and other invertebrates. In the Gulf of Maine, herring is a primary food.

## When is an Atlantic cod in season?

Cod are a cold-water groundfish present in the Northeast year-round in suitable depths, moving shallower and deeper seasonally. Cod are tightly regulated due to depleted stocks, and seasons, size limits, and bag limits set by NOAA Fisheries and state agencies change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an Atlantic cod spawn?

Atlantic cod are broadcast spawners that release pelagic (free-drifting) eggs into the water column, typically over winter and spring on traditional spawning grounds, with timing varying by stock and region. A single large female can produce enormous numbers of eggs. Eggs and larvae drift with currents before juveniles settle to the bottom in nursery habitat. Cod often return to the same spawning grounds, and some stocks form large spawning aggregations.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Atlantic cod are a marine groundfish of the cold North Atlantic, living near the bottom on the continental shelf and in coastal waters.

## Fun fact / angler note

Atlantic cod were so central to New England's history that the "sacred cod" hangs in the Massachusetts State House, and the Cape Cod peninsula is named for them. Because many cod stocks are depleted and strictly managed, anglers should pay especially close attention to current regulations before keeping one.

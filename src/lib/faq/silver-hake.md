# Silver Hake (Merluccius bilinearis)

Also known as: whiting, Atlantic whiting.

## What is a silver hake?

The silver hake, commonly sold as "whiting," is a slender, fast-swimming North Atlantic fish in the merluccid hake family, found along the continental shelf from Newfoundland to the Carolinas, abundant in the Gulf of Maine and on Georges Bank. It is silvery, with a large mouth full of sharp teeth and two dorsal fins, and it is a more active, predatory swimmer than the bottom-bound Urophycis hakes. Silver hake are an important commercial species and a frequent inshore catch.

## What does a silver hake eat?

Silver hake are aggressive predators. They feed heavily on small fish — including herring, sand lance, and even smaller silver hake — along with shrimp, krill, squid, and other crustaceans. They are known for chasing prey up off the bottom and into shallow water, sometimes even stranding themselves on beaches while pursuing baitfish.

## When is a silver hake in season?

Silver hake are present in New England waters year-round in suitable depths and often move inshore at certain times of year, which is when they are most accessible to anglers. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and regional councils, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a silver hake spawn?

Silver hake are broadcast spawners that release pelagic, drifting eggs into the water column. Spawning occurs mostly from spring through summer over the continental shelf and in the Gulf of Maine, with timing varying by area. Eggs and larvae drift before the young settle into nursery habitat.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Silver hake are a marine species of the continental shelf and Gulf of Maine, from inshore waters to deeper offshore grounds.

## Fun fact / angler note

Silver hake are such determined predators that they sometimes chase baitfish right onto the beach in a feeding frenzy. As "whiting," they're popular eating, with soft, mild flesh — best handled and iced quickly because they soften fast. Their toothy mouths warrant care when unhooking.

# Fallfish (Semotilus corporalis)

Also known as: fallfish, eastern chub, silver chub, corporal, windfish.

## What is a fallfish?

The fallfish is the largest native minnow in the northeastern United States — a streamlined, silvery fish that can reach well over a foot long, big for a member of the minnow family (Cyprinidae). It is bright silver with large scales and a slightly darker back, and breeding males develop small tubercles ("pearl organs") on the head and a rosy tint. Fallfish live in clear streams, rivers, and lakes with good water quality, often in the same cool, clean water as trout. They're an aggressive, willing biter and a surprisingly strong fighter for their family, sometimes caught by trout and bass anglers.

## What does a fallfish eat?

Fallfish are opportunistic feeders. They eat aquatic and terrestrial insects, larvae, small crustaceans, worms, and small fish, taking food from the drift, the surface, and the bottom. Their willingness to chase insects and small lures is why fly and spin anglers fishing for trout often hook them.

## When is a fallfish in season?

Fallfish are caught spring through fall and are active warm-season biters in clean streams and rivers. They're usually managed under general fishing rules (often as a nongame species) set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations.

## When does a fallfish spawn?

Fallfish are famous nest builders. In spring, the male carries pebbles and small stones in his mouth to construct a large mound-shaped gravel nest — sometimes several feet across — in the stream, one of the largest nests built by any North American fish relative to its size. The eggs are deposited in and protected by the stone mound. These conspicuous gravel mounds in clean streams are a fallfish signature.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Fallfish live in clear, good-quality freshwater streams, rivers, and lakes.

## Fun fact / angler note

For its size, the fallfish builds one of the most impressive nests of any freshwater fish — the male hauls pebbles one at a time in his mouth to pile up a gravel mound that can be several feet across and a foot or more high. Anglers chasing trout in clean streams often catch fallfish by accident and are surprised by the fight from "just a minnow" — the largest native minnow in the Northeast.

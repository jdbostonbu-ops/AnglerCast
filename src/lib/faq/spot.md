# Spot (Leiostomus xanthurus)

Also known as: spot croaker, Norfolk spot, flat croaker.

## What is a spot?

The spot is a small, deep-bodied member of the drum family (Sciaenidae), named for the distinct dark spot just behind each gill cover. It is a coastal and estuarine fish ranging along the U.S. Atlantic coast from roughly Massachusetts to the Gulf of Mexico, most common in the Mid-Atlantic and South. Spot travel in schools over sand and mud bottoms in bays, estuaries, and nearshore waters, and they are a popular, easy-to-catch panfish and a widely used bait species.

## What does a spot eat?

Spot are bottom feeders that root through soft sediment for small invertebrates. Their diet includes copepods, amphipods, small worms (polychaetes), nematodes, small mollusks, tiny crustaceans, and organic detritus. They feed close to the bottom in estuaries and coastal shallows.

## When is a spot in season?

Spot are a warm-season inshore presence in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic, moving into bays and estuaries in the warmer months and offshore to spawn as it cools. Seasons and limits, where they apply, are set by state agencies and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and can change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a spot spawn?

Spot spawn offshore in coastal ocean waters, mainly in fall and winter as adults move out of the estuaries. The eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift, and the young then move into estuaries, which serve as important nursery grounds where juveniles feed and grow before returning to the coast. Spot are relatively short-lived and fast-growing.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Spot are a coastal marine and estuarine species; they depend heavily on estuaries as nursery habitat but spawn in the ocean and live in salt and brackish water.

## Fun fact / angler note

Spot are a favorite of family and pier anglers because they school thickly and bite simple bottom rigs, and the "spot run" in fall is a well-known event in the Mid-Atlantic. They're also one of the most popular live baits for larger predators like red drum and striped bass.

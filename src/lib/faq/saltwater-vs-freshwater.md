# Saltwater vs Freshwater

AnglerCast splits its species data and search tools into Saltwater and Freshwater pages because the two are genuinely different sports. The water is different. The gear is different. The species are different. The way you plan a trip is different. Knowing which one you are doing — and using the matching page in the app — is the first decision before any of the others.

## The basic difference

Freshwater is inland water with very low salinity — lakes, ponds, rivers, streams, reservoirs. Saltwater is the ocean and the bays, estuaries, and tidal rivers connected to it. Brackish water (where fresh and salt mix, like the mouth of a river) is its own thing and is usually treated as saltwater for licensing and species purposes. AnglerCast follows that convention — saltwater queries hit GBIF for marine species and pull tide and marine conditions, while freshwater queries hit GBIF for inland species and pull USGS streamgage data and forecast conditions.

## Different species

The species lists on AnglerCast's Saltwater and Freshwater pages reflect what actually lives in each environment. Saltwater includes species like striped bass, bluefish, summer flounder (fluke), black sea bass, tautog, Atlantic cod, bluefin and yellowfin tuna, mahi-mahi, swordfish, and many more — fish built for moving water, big runs, and offshore endurance. Freshwater includes largemouth and smallmouth bass, brook and rainbow trout, walleye, northern pike, lake trout, panfish like bluegill and crappie, channel catfish, and the rest of the inland lineup.

Some species — striped bass, American eel, rainbow smelt — appear on both lists because they move between saltwater and freshwater during their life cycle. If you are not sure which page to search, start with the water type you are actually fishing.

## Different gear

Saltwater gear is built to survive the corrosive effect of salt. Reels are sealed or made from materials that resist rust; rods use stainless or corrosion-resistant guides; line is heavier; lures are larger and tougher. Freshwater gear is generally lighter, finer, and more varied — sensitive spinning rods for trout, baitcasting setups for bass, fly rods for streams. Saltwater gear can sometimes be used in freshwater; the reverse usually destroys the equipment fast. If you are starting out, do not bring your bass rod into the surf and expect it to survive the day.

## Different conditions

Freshwater conditions are relatively stable. Water temperature changes slowly across a season, streamflow changes after rain or snowmelt but stays in a predictable range, and there are no tides to plan around. Saltwater conditions are much more dynamic. Tides move the water in and out twice a day, currents shift with the tide and the wind, water temperature can change quickly with upwelling or coastal weather, and offshore weather can change in hours.

This is why AnglerCast pulls different live data for each: tide predictions (NOAA) and marine wave/swell/current data (Open-Meteo Marine) for saltwater destinations; streamflow, gage height, and water temperature (USGS) for freshwater destinations. Wind and forecast data are pulled for both because wind matters everywhere.

## Different planning

Saltwater trips usually require more planning. You time around the tide change, you check the marine forecast, you make sure your boat and gear can handle the conditions you will face offshore, and you carry safety equipment appropriate to the distance from shore. Freshwater trips are often shorter and more spontaneous — a lake or river close to home, lighter gear, less weather risk. Saltwater fishing can also be more physically demanding because the fish are larger and the fight is longer. Neither is harder in an absolute sense; they are demanding in different ways.

## Using the right page in AnglerCast

When you open AnglerCast, pick Saltwater if you are fishing the ocean, a bay, a tidal river, or an estuary, and Freshwater if you are fishing a lake, pond, river, or stream that does not carry tide. Each page is configured for its environment: the species dropdown, the conditions data, the AI explanation context. Using the right page gets you the right answer.

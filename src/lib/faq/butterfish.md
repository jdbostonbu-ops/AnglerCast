# Butterfish (Peprilus triacanthus)

Also known as: butterfish, dollarfish, harvestfish (related species), shiner.

## What is a butterfish?

The butterfish is a small, deep-bodied, silvery schooling fish with a thin, almost disc-like profile, found along the U.S. Atlantic coast from the Gulf of St. Lawrence to Florida, common in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic. Its name comes from its soft, oily, rich flesh. Butterfish travel in schools over the continental shelf and in coastal waters, often near the surface or mid-water, and they are an important forage fish for many larger predators as well as a commercial species.

## What does a butterfish eat?

Butterfish are plankton and small-prey feeders. They eat zooplankton, small jellyfish and comb jellies, small crustaceans, worms, and tiny fish. They feed in the water column as they move with their schools.

## When is a butterfish in season?

Butterfish are present in Northeast coastal and shelf waters and move seasonally — inshore as it warms, offshore as it cools. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and regional councils, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a butterfish spawn?

Butterfish are broadcast spawners that release pelagic (drifting) eggs into the water column, generally spawning from spring into summer in coastal and shelf waters. Eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift, and the young grow quickly. Butterfish are short-lived and fast-growing. Young butterfish are well known for sheltering under the bells of jellyfish for protection.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Butterfish are a coastal and shelf marine schooling species, living in the water column rather than on the bottom.

## Fun fact / angler note

Young butterfish often shelter beneath the bells of large jellyfish, darting in among the stinging tentacles for protection from predators. Their rich, oily flesh makes them excellent eating and also a prime cut bait for striped bass, bluefish, and tuna. Anglers most often encounter them as bait schools that draw in larger gamefish.

# Common Carp (Cyprinus carpio)

Also known as: carp, European carp, German carp, mirror carp / leather carp (scale varieties).

## What is a common carp?

The common carp is a large, hardy, heavy-bodied member of the minnow family (Cyprinidae), native to Europe and Asia and introduced to North America over a century ago. It has a deep, thick golden-brown to bronze body, large scales, a pair of barbels at each corner of the mouth, and a long dorsal fin. Carp thrive in warm, slow, often murky or nutrient-rich (eutrophic) waters — lakes, ponds, and slow rivers — and tolerate conditions that exclude many native fish. They're considered invasive in much of North America because their bottom-rooting feeding stirs up sediment and degrades water quality and waterfowl habitat. Despite that, they grow very large and fight hard, and a dedicated carp-angling community prizes them.

## What does a common carp eat?

Common carp are omnivorous bottom feeders. They root through soft sediment for aquatic insects and larvae, crustaceans, worms, mollusks, snails, plant material, seeds, and detritus, using their barbels to taste and find food. This rooting behavior is exactly what makes them ecologically disruptive — it muddies the water and uproots aquatic plants.

## When is a common carp in season?

Carp are caught spring through fall, with warm months most productive; they feed actively and often in groups from dusk to dawn. They're typically not restrictively regulated (and in many places are considered a nuisance or are even targeted for removal), but rules vary by state and are subject to change. Always check current local regulations.

## When does a common carp spawn?

Common carp spawn in late spring and early summer, when water warms, moving into shallow, weedy, flooded areas. They are extremely prolific — a single large female can release hundreds of thousands to over a million eggs — broadcasting sticky eggs over vegetation with no nest and no parental care. This enormous reproductive output is a big reason carp can quickly dominate a water body.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Common carp live in warm freshwater lakes, ponds, and slow rivers (tolerating slightly brackish water in some places).

## Fun fact / angler note

Often dismissed as a "trash fish" in North America, the common carp is one of the most prized sportfish in Europe, where anglers pursue trophy carp with specialized tackle and techniques — and that carp-fishing culture has a growing following here too. They grow big, run hard, and are surprisingly wary and intelligent. (Ecologically, though, they're invasive in North America, so many fisheries encourage removing rather than releasing them — check local rules.)

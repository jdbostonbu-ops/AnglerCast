# Rock Bass (Ambloplites rupestris)

Also known as: rock bass, goggle-eye, redeye, rock sunfish.

## What is a rock bass?

The rock bass is a chunky, structure-loving member of the sunfish family, easily recognized by its bright red eyes and rows of dark spots along brassy-green sides. Though it resembles a small smallmouth in body shape, it is usually quite a bit smaller, averaging 6–10 inches and rarely topping a pound. Rock bass live in clear, rocky lakes and cool streams, often holding around rocks, logs, and other cover near smallmouth bass. They are aggressive biters and a fun, accessible panfish, especially for younger anglers.

## What does a rock bass eat?

Rock bass are carnivorous and eat a varied diet of insects, aquatic crustaceans (including crayfish), and smaller fish — including minnows, young yellow perch, and even their own young at times. They feed most heavily in the evening and early morning and will occasionally take food off the surface. Their large mouth lets them eat surprisingly big prey for their size.

## When is a rock bass in season?

Rock bass are caught spring through fall and are active, willing biters in warm water. They generally don't have restrictive special seasons, but are often included under general panfish or sunfish regulations set by state agencies. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a rock bass spawn?

Rock bass spawn in late spring to early summer, generally April into June, when water warms to roughly 54–59°F. Like other sunfish, the male digs a nest in the shallows near shore and aggressively guards the eggs and fry. A female can lay from a couple thousand to over ten thousand eggs. They mature at about 2–3 years old and few live beyond 10–12 years.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Rock bass live in clear, rocky freshwater lakes and cool streams.

## Fun fact / angler note

The rock bass's nickname "goggle-eye" comes from its big, bright red eyes — a quick way to identify it. It can also change color rapidly, going lighter or darker and showing its blotchy pattern depending on mood and surroundings. Rock bass are eager biters that often share rocky habitat with smallmouth, so anglers frequently catch them while bass fishing — and they're a great, scrappy target for kids.

# Atlantic Salmon (Salmo salar)

Also known as: salmon, "the king of fish," landlocked salmon / ouananiche (landlocked form).

## What is an Atlantic salmon?

The Atlantic salmon is a legendary cold-water gamefish, anadromous by nature — born in fresh water, migrating to the ocean to grow, and returning to its home river to spawn. It is sleek and silvery at sea, with a few dark spots, developing darker colors and (in males) a hooked jaw at spawning time. Wild sea-run Atlantic salmon in U.S. rivers are now rare and federally protected, the focus of major restoration efforts, but there are also landlocked Atlantic salmon (sometimes called ouananiche) that live their whole lives in fresh water in lakes like those of Maine and the Northeast, where they're a prized sportfish that chases smelt.

## What does an Atlantic salmon eat?

Diet changes with life stage. Young salmon (parr) in the river eat aquatic and terrestrial insects, larvae, and small invertebrates. At sea (or in a large lake, for landlocked fish), they become fish-eaters, feeding heavily on baitfish such as herring, capelin, sand lance, and — for landlocked salmon — rainbow smelt, which is their signature forage. Returning sea-run adults eat little once they enter the river to spawn.

## When is an Atlantic salmon in season?

Wild sea-run Atlantic salmon in U.S. rivers are protected, and targeting them is generally prohibited where they're endangered — always check, as many runs are closed to protect the fish. Landlocked Atlantic salmon are a managed sportfish with seasons and limits set by state agencies (spring and fall are prime), and these change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an Atlantic salmon spawn?

Atlantic salmon spawn in fall (generally October–November), in clean, cold, gravel-bottomed rivers and streams. The female digs a redd in the gravel and buries the fertilized eggs, which incubate over winter and hatch in spring. Unlike Pacific salmon, Atlantic salmon do not always die after spawning — some survive and return to spawn again. Landlocked salmon run up tributary streams of their lakes to spawn the same way.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both, depending on the population. Sea-run Atlantic salmon are anadromous — they spawn in fresh water and grow in the ocean. Landlocked Atlantic salmon complete their entire life cycle in fresh water (lakes and their tributary streams), which is why the species appears in a freshwater context in the Northeast.

## Fun fact / angler note

Atlantic salmon are often called "the king of fish," and unlike Pacific salmon they can survive spawning and return to do it again. The landlocked form is essentially the same fish that never goes to sea — it grows fat chasing rainbow smelt in cold northern lakes, and a spring landlocked salmon on light tackle is a classic Northeast prize. Wild sea-run salmon, by contrast, are now so rare in U.S. rivers that they're protected and the subject of intensive restoration.

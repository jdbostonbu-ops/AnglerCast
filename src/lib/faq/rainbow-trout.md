# Rainbow Trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss)

Also known as: rainbow, 'bow, redband; steelhead (sea-run/lake-run form).

## What is a rainbow trout?

The rainbow trout is a popular, hard-fighting cold-water gamefish native to western North America and the Pacific drainage, introduced and widely stocked across the Northeast and the rest of the country. It is silvery with a scattering of small black spots and a distinctive pink-to-red stripe running along each side, which gives it its name. Rainbow trout are prized for their acrobatic fight — they often leap when hooked — and they thrive in cold streams, rivers, and lakes. A migratory form that runs to large lakes or the sea and returns to spawn is called a "steelhead."

## What does a rainbow trout eat?

Rainbow trout are opportunistic feeders. They eat aquatic and terrestrial insects, larvae, crustaceans, worms, fish eggs, and small fish. Insects make up a large part of the diet, especially in younger fish, which is why rainbows are a favorite fly-fishing target; larger fish take more small fish and crayfish.

## When is a rainbow trout in season?

Rainbow trout are a cold-water species most active in cooler conditions; in the Northeast they're stocked and targeted heavily in spring and fall and seek cooler water in summer. Seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by state fish and wildlife agencies and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a rainbow trout spawn?

Unlike the fall-spawning brook and brown trout, rainbow trout spawn in spring (generally late winter into spring as water warms). The female digs a gravel redd in clean, oxygenated water, and the eggs hatch in a few weeks to a couple of months depending on temperature. Many Northeast rainbow populations are maintained by stocking, since natural reproduction requires the right cold, clean, gravel-bottomed streams.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Rainbow trout live in cold freshwater streams, rivers, and lakes. (The sea-run form, the steelhead, spends part of its life in salt water on the Pacific coast, but Northeast rainbows are a freshwater fish.)

## Fun fact / angler note

A rainbow trout and a steelhead are the same species — "steelhead" is just the name for rainbows that migrate to the ocean (or a big lake) and grow large before returning to spawn. Rainbows are famous for their leaping, runs-and-jumps fight, which makes them a favorite of both fly and spin anglers, and they're one of the most widely stocked gamefish in the country.

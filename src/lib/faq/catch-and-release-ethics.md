# Catch and Release Ethics

Catch and release is the practice of immediately returning a fish to the water where it was caught. NOAA Fisheries calls it a great conservation strategy, but with a critical caveat: simply letting a fish go does not guarantee it will live. The actions you take before, during, and after landing the fish are what actually determine whether it survives.

If you are going to practice catch and release, do it well. A fish that swims off looking healthy and dies an hour later from gill damage or exhaustion is no better off than one in the cooler — and worse for the fishery, because no one is even tracking the loss.

## Know before you go

Match your tackle to the species you are targeting so you can land the fish quickly. Underpowered gear means a long fight and an exhausted fish, which dramatically lowers survival odds even when the release looks fine. Bring extra hooks, line, long-nose pliers, wire cutters, and a quality landing net. NOAA recommends a wet, soft knotless mesh or rubber net because it does less damage to the fish's eyes, fins, scales, and protective mucus coating than traditional knotted nets.

Know the regulations for your water. Size limits, slot limits, bag limits, and protected species rules exist for a reason, and "if you don't know, let it go" is the safest position.

## Gear choices that improve survival

Hooks matter more than almost anything else. Circle hooks, barbless hooks, or hooks with crimped barbs all increase survival rates and make removal faster. Circle hooks tend to lodge in the corner of the mouth rather than deep in the throat — a Florida study on snappers and groupers found catch rates were the same with circle hooks as with traditional J-hooks, while injury rates dropped substantially.

Non-stainless steel hooks corrode and fall out over time if a fish escapes still hooked. Stainless hooks do not. For catch-and-release fishing, choose corrodible hooks.

Carry a dehooker or release tool. The less you handle the fish, the better.

## Fighting the fish

Never fight a fish to exhaustion. A fish that comes to the boat or the bank limp is a fish that may die regardless of how carefully you release it. Match your gear to your target and apply enough pressure to land the fish in a reasonable amount of time. If you cannot, scale up your tackle.

## Handling

Wet your hands before touching a fish. Dry hands strip the protective mucus coating that defends against bacterial and fungal infection.

Avoid the gills. The gill filaments are how the fish breathes — touching them, squeezing them, or letting a hook tear through gill tissue causes bleeding that is often fatal. Handle the fish from the lip or the belly only. Never lift a large fish vertically by the jaw alone; support its body weight with a hand under the belly.

Minimize air exposure. Every second out of water is a second the fish is not breathing. NOAA's guidance is to dehook the fish in the water whenever possible, and to keep the fish in the water until a camera is ready if you want a photo. Three to five seconds out of water for a photo is reasonable; thirty seconds is not.

Never gaff a fish you plan to release.

If a hook is deep and cannot be easily removed without serious injury, cut the line as close to the hook as possible and release the fish with the hook still in it. A corrodible hook will eventually rust out and the fish will heal. A fish whose throat has been torn apart trying to extract the hook will not.

## Reviving and releasing

A healthy fish will swim off on its own when you place it back in the water headfirst. A fish that does not — one that is lethargic, floating sideways, or motionless — needs help.

Hold the fish gently in the water, headfirst into any current, and let water pass through its gills. Cradle it without squeezing until it kicks off under its own power. This can take 30 seconds for a healthy fish and several minutes for an exhausted one. Do not throw a fish back; lower it gently.

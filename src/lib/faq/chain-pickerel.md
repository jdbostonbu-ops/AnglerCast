# Chain Pickerel (Esox niger)

Also known as: pickerel, chain pike, jack, southern pike, grass pike.

## What is a chain pickerel?

The chain pickerel is the smallest of the common pike-family gamefish in the Northeast, a slender, aggressive ambush predator named for the bold, dark chain-link pattern over its greenish-gold sides. Like its larger pike and muskie relatives, it has a duckbill snout and sharp teeth, with a fully scaled cheek (a key ID feature). Chain pickerel live in weedy, slow waters — ponds, lakes, swamps, and quiet river backwaters — holding in vegetation to ambush prey. They reach up to around 24 inches and a couple of pounds, are willing biters, and are a fun, scrappy catch, including through the ice.

## What does a chain pickerel eat?

Chain pickerel are aggressive, fish-eating ambush predators. They feed mainly on small fish (minnows, sunfish, yellow perch, and others), along with crayfish, frogs, large insects, and the occasional small creature. They lie motionless in the weeds and strike with a fast lunge, taking surprisingly large prey for their size.

## When is a chain pickerel in season?

Chain pickerel are caught year-round and are a popular cold-water and ice-fishing target when other species slow down. They're often managed under general or pickerel-specific rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a chain pickerel spawn?

Chain pickerel spawn in early spring (and sometimes again in fall in parts of the range), moving into weedy, flooded shallows. Like other pikes, they scatter adhesive eggs over vegetation with no nest and no parental care, and the eggs stick to plants until they hatch. They are among the earlier spring spawners.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Chain pickerel live in weedy freshwater ponds, lakes, swamps, and slow rivers (tolerating slightly brackish coastal water in some areas).

## Fun fact / angler note

The "chain" name is literal — the dark, interlocking chain-link pattern on its sides is the easiest way to identify this fish. Chain pickerel are often caught when little else is biting, especially through the ice, and despite being bony they put up a feisty, slashing fight far above their weight. Use a leader: like all pikes, they have teeth that can cut light line.

# Bowfin (Amia calva)

Also known as: dogfish, mudfish, grinnel, grindle, swamp bass, cypress trout.

## What is a bowfin?

The bowfin is a living fossil — the only surviving member of an ancient order of fish (Amiiformes) that dates back more than 150 million years. It has a long, cylindrical, heavily built body, a very long undulating dorsal fin running most of its back, a bony head, and a distinctive dark "eyespot" near the tail (more prominent in males). Bowfin live in warm, weedy, slow or still fresh waters — vegetated bays, swamps, sluggish rivers, and backwaters. One of their most remarkable traits is the ability to breathe air: they gulp air into a lung-like swim bladder, letting them survive in warm, low-oxygen water that would suffocate most fish, even in nearly dry, mucky conditions for a time.

## What does a bowfin eat?

Bowfin are voracious, opportunistic top predators. They feed mainly on fish, along with crayfish (a favorite), frogs, large insects, and other invertebrates. They ambush prey from cover in the weeds and strike hard, playing a real role as a predator in their systems despite an old reputation as a "nuisance fish."

## When is a bowfin in season?

Bowfin are caught spring through fall and bite aggressively in warm water. They've historically had few specific regulations (and were often dismissed as rough fish), but they're increasingly recognized as a gamefish — including for bowfishing and a growing caviar fishery — so rules vary by state and are subject to change. Always check current local regulations.

## When does a bowfin spawn?

Bowfin spawn in spring, and unusually for a fish like this, the male provides devoted parental care. The male builds and clears a nest in shallow, vegetated water, then guards the eggs and the resulting school of young for up to a couple of months — bowfin fry form a dense, dark ball that the father actively protects. This long parental guarding is a notable trait.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Bowfin live in warm, weedy, slow freshwater — swamps, vegetated bays, sluggish rivers, and backwaters.

## Fun fact / angler note

The bowfin is a true living fossil and an air-breather — it can gulp atmospheric air into a lung-like swim bladder and survive where almost nothing else can, even in mud with little standing water. Long scorned as a "trash fish," bowfin are now winning respect as a hard-fighting gamefish (they're brutally strong on the line) and are slow-growing and long-lived, with some fish topping 30 years — a reason to handle them with care.

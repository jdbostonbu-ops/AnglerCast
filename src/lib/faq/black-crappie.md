# Black Crappie (Pomoxis nigromaculatus)

Also known as: crappie, calico bass, speckled perch, papermouth, specks.

## What is a black crappie?

The black crappie is a popular, deep-bodied panfish in the sunfish family, prized as one of the best-eating freshwater fish. It is silvery with an irregular scattering of dark black speckles and blotches (no neat vertical bars), distinguishing it from the white crappie. Black crappie favor clearer, weedier, cooler water than white crappie and live in lakes, ponds, reservoirs, and slow rivers, schooling around brush, weeds, docks, and submerged timber. They have soft, papery mouths (hence "papermouth") and are a hugely popular spring and ice-fishing target.

## What does a black crappie eat?

Black crappie eat zooplankton and insects when young, shifting to a diet of small fish (especially minnows and small shad) and aquatic insects and larvae as adults. They feed heavily at dawn and dusk, often suspending in open water near cover and ambushing baitfish schools.

## When is a black crappie in season?

Crappie are caught year-round, with the spring pre-spawn and spawn being the most popular and productive time, and they're a top ice-fishing species in winter. They're managed under panfish or crappie-specific rules (sometimes with size and bag limits) set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a black crappie spawn?

Black crappie spawn in spring, generally when water reaches about 58–68°F, moving into shallow, weedy or brushy areas. The male sweeps out a nest and guards the eggs and fry, much like other sunfish, often nesting in loose colonies near cover. Spring spawning concentrations in the shallows are what make crappie so catchable that time of year.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Black crappie live in clear, weedy freshwater lakes, ponds, reservoirs, and slow rivers.

## Fun fact / angler note

"Papermouth" is a warning as much as a nickname — a crappie's mouth membrane is thin and tears easily, so anglers avoid horsing them in or setting the hook too hard, or the hook pulls free. Crappie school tightly around brush and timber, so when you find one you usually find many, and a spring crappie fry is one of the great traditions of panfishing.

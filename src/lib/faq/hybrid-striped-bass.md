# Hybrid Striped Bass (Morone saxatilis × chrysops)

Also known as: wiper, whiterock bass, sunshine bass, Cherokee bass, hybrid striper.

## What is a hybrid striped bass?

The hybrid striped bass is a cross between the striped bass and the white bass, produced in hatcheries and widely stocked in lakes and reservoirs as a hard-fighting sportfish. It combines traits of both parents: the size and power of the striped bass with the hardiness and reservoir-adaptability of the white bass. It looks like a stockier striped bass, silvery with dark horizontal stripes — but the stripes are typically broken or offset rather than clean and continuous, which is a key way to tell it from a pure striped bass. Hybrids grow fast, school aggressively, and tolerate the warm, open water of reservoirs better than pure stripers, making them a popular managed gamefish.

## What does a hybrid striped bass eat?

Hybrid striped bass are voracious, schooling, open-water predators. They feed heavily on baitfish — especially shad (gizzard and threadfin), herring, and other schooling fish — chasing bait schools and often driving them to the surface in feeding frenzies. They also eat crayfish and large insects. Their aggressive, fast-paced feeding is part of what makes them exciting to catch.

## When is a hybrid striped bass in season?

Hybrids are caught spring through fall, with explosive surface "boils" on shad schools a prized summer and fall event, plus good cold-water action. As a stocked, managed gamefish they typically carry specific size and bag limits set by state agencies, which change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a hybrid striped bass spawn?

Hybrid striped bass generally do not reproduce successfully in the wild — the hybrid is largely sterile or reproductively ineffective, so populations are maintained almost entirely by hatchery production and stocking. (Where any reproduction or back-crossing occurs it's minor and not what sustains the fishery.) This is actually useful for managers: stocked hybrids provide a powerful predator and sportfish without establishing a self-sustaining population that could overrun a reservoir.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater (as stocked). Hybrid striped bass are stocked and fished in freshwater lakes and reservoirs. Their parent species include the anadromous striped bass (which lives in salt water and spawns in fresh) and the freshwater white bass, but the hybrid itself is managed as a freshwater reservoir gamefish.

## Fun fact / angler note

Anglers often call them "wipers," and they're famous for their brawling, no-quit fight — pound for pound among the hardest-pulling fish in fresh water. The tell-tale ID is the stripes: a hybrid's lateral stripes are usually broken or staggered, while a pure striped bass has clean, unbroken lines. Because they're stocked and largely can't reproduce, wiper fisheries are carefully managed to give anglers a big, aggressive predator without ecological overpopulation.

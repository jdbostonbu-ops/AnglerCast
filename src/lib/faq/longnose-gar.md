# Longnose Gar (Lepisosteus osseus)

Also known as: gar, garpike, needlenose gar, billy gar.

## What is a longnose gar?

The longnose gar is an ancient, primitive fish instantly recognized by its long, slender, beak-like snout lined with needle-sharp teeth. It has a long, cylindrical, torpedo-shaped body armored with hard, diamond-shaped (ganoid) scales, and the dorsal and anal fins set far back near the tail. Gar are a living link to deep evolutionary history (the gar family goes back over 100 million years), found in warm, slow rivers, lakes, backwaters, and reservoirs. Like the bowfin, gar can gulp air into a lung-like swim bladder, letting them survive warm, low-oxygen water. They often hang motionless near the surface, basking and ambushing prey.

## What does a longnose gar eat?

Longnose gar are ambush predators specialized for catching fish. They feed almost entirely on other fish, lying still or drifting near the surface and then seizing prey with a quick sideways slash of their tooth-filled snout. They also take crustaceans and large insects at times. The long, toothy beak is the perfect tool for grabbing slippery baitfish.

## When is a longnose gar in season?

Longnose gar are caught spring through fall, biting most in warm water. Regulations vary by state (gar have historically been lightly regulated but are increasingly recognized as sportfish, including for bowfishing) and are subject to change. Always check current local regulations.

## When does a longnose gar spawn?

Longnose gar spawn in spring and early summer, moving into shallow, weedy, or flooded areas, often over vegetation or gravel. They scatter their eggs with no nest and no parental care. Notably, gar eggs are toxic to many would-be predators (including humans and other animals), which helps protect the developing eggs. The young grow quickly.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Longnose gar live in warm freshwater rivers, lakes, backwaters, and reservoirs (tolerating slightly brackish water in some coastal areas).

## Fun fact / angler note

Longnose gar are armored air-breathers from the age of dinosaurs — their hard, enamel-like ganoid scales are so tough that Native peoples used gar skin and scales as tools and armor, and the fish can breathe air to survive stagnant water. A word of caution: gar eggs are poisonous if eaten, a natural defense for the species. On the line, gar are powerful and acrobatic — and notoriously hard to hook because of that bony beak, which is why specialized rope-fiber lures (that tangle in the teeth) are sometimes used.

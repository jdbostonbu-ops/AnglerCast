# Brown Bullhead (Ameiurus nebulosus)

Also known as: bullhead, brown bullhead catfish, horned pout, mudcat, catfish.

## What is a brown bullhead?

The brown bullhead is a small, hardy catfish native to the eastern U.S. and widely introduced, common in ponds, lakes, and slow rivers throughout the Northeast. It is a scaleless, smooth-skinned fish with whisker-like barbels (dark chin barbels distinguish it from the yellow bullhead), a mottled brown-to-olive body, and — unlike channel catfish — a squared-off rather than forked tail. Brown bullheads tolerate warm, muddy, low-oxygen water that many fish can't handle, and they feed mostly at night by smell and taste. They're an accessible, willing catch and good eating from clean water.

## What does a brown bullhead eat?

Brown bullheads are bottom-feeding omnivores and scavengers. They eat aquatic insects and larvae, worms, crayfish, snails, small fish, fish eggs, plant material, algae, and detritus, locating food by touch, smell, and taste with their sensitive barbels. They feed largely after dark, rooting along the bottom.

## When is a brown bullhead in season?

Brown bullheads are caught spring through fall, with warm evenings especially productive. They're usually managed under general or catfish/panfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a brown bullhead spawn?

Brown bullheads spawn in late spring and early summer in shallow water. One or both parents fan out a nest in mud or sand near cover, and after the eggs are laid the parents guard them and then the school of young — bullhead parents are notably protective, sometimes seen herding a tight black "ball" of fry through the shallows.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Brown bullheads live in warm freshwater ponds, lakes, and slow rivers, tolerating muddy, low-oxygen conditions.

## Fun fact / angler note

Watch for a swirling black cloud in a pond shallows in early summer — it may be a school of bullhead fry being shepherded by a guarding parent, an unusually devoted bit of fish parenting. The old name "horned pout" refers to the sharp pectoral and dorsal spines, which can jab you, so handle them with care. Dark chin barbels are the quick way to tell a brown bullhead from a yellow bullhead.

# Acadian Redfish (Sebastes fasciatus)

Also known as: redfish, ocean perch, Atlantic ocean perch, rosefish, brim.

## What is an Acadian redfish?

The Acadian redfish is a bright orange-red, deepwater rockfish of the cold North Atlantic, found in the Gulf of Maine and on offshore banks over deep, rocky and muddy bottoms. It has large eyes (an adaptation to dim, deep water), a spiny dorsal fin, and the slow life history typical of rockfishes — very slow growth and long life (redfish can live for decades). It is marketed as "ocean perch." After a long period of depletion, Gulf of Maine redfish recovered and became a managed groundfish success story.

## What does an Acadian redfish eat?

Acadian redfish are deepwater predators that feed on small fish, krill, shrimp, copepods, and other crustaceans, along with squid. They feed in the water column and near the bottom in deep water, taking advantage of concentrations of small prey.

## When is an Acadian redfish in season?

Redfish are a deepwater groundfish present in the Gulf of Maine year-round in suitable depths. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and the New England Fishery Management Council, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an Acadian redfish spawn?

Unlike most fish in this list, Acadian redfish bear live young. Fertilization is internal, and the female releases tiny live larvae into the water column (ovoviviparous), generally in spring and summer. The combination of slow growth, late maturity, and small numbers of young makes redfish populations slow to rebuild — which is why their recovery in the Gulf of Maine is considered a notable management success.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Acadian redfish are a deepwater marine rockfish of the cold Gulf of Maine and offshore North Atlantic banks.

## Fun fact / angler note

Acadian redfish are one of the longest-lived fish a New England angler might encounter — they grow extremely slowly and can live for several decades, so a modest-sized "ocean perch" may be surprisingly old. Their large eyes are a giveaway of the deep, dim water they call home. They're prized as a mild, flaky table fish.

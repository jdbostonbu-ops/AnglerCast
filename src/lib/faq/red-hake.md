# Red Hake (Urophycis chuss)

Also known as: ling, squirrel hake, mud hake, cock hake, butter mullet.

## What is a red hake?

The red hake is a soft-bodied bottom-dwelling fish in the phycid hake group, closely related to white hake but generally smaller and more reddish-brown. It ranges along the North Atlantic shelf from Newfoundland to North Carolina, common in the Gulf of Maine and the Mid-Atlantic. Like white hake, it has an elongated body, a long second dorsal and anal fin, a chin barbel, and thread-like pelvic fins used to feel along the bottom. Red hake favor soft mud bottoms and depressions and are a commercial and incidental recreational species.

## What does a red hake eat?

Red hake are bottom predators that feed on crustaceans — shrimp, krill, amphipods, crabs — along with small fish and squid. They use their sensitive feeler fins and barbel to find prey on and just above the muddy bottom. Diet shifts toward more fish as they grow.

## When is a red hake in season?

Red hake are present in New England and Mid-Atlantic waters year-round in suitable depths, moving inshore and offshore seasonally. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and regional councils, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a red hake spawn?

Red hake are broadcast spawners that release pelagic, drifting eggs into the water column, generally spawning from spring through fall depending on the area (with notable summer spawning in the Mid-Atlantic and Gulf of Maine). Eggs and larvae drift, and young red hake have a famous habit of sheltering inside live sea scallops as juveniles before settling to the bottom.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Red hake are a marine groundfish living on soft mud bottoms along the continental shelf and in the Gulf of Maine.

## Fun fact / angler note

Juvenile red hake are remarkable for sheltering inside the shells of live sea scallops — a tiny hake will live in the scallop's mantle cavity for protection until it outgrows the shelter. Often called "ling," red hake are caught incidentally on bottom rigs meant for cod and other groundfish.

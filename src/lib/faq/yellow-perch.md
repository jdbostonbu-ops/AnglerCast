# Yellow Perch (Perca flavescens)

Also known as: perch, lake perch, ringed perch, American perch, raccoon perch.

## What is a yellow perch?

The yellow perch is a popular, abundant freshwater panfish recognized by its golden-yellow sides crossed by six to eight dark vertical bars and its orange-tinted lower fins. It belongs to the perch family (along with the walleye and sauger) and lives in ponds, lakes, and slow-flowing rivers, most often in clear water near vegetation. Yellow perch travel in schools, are active during the day, and are a favorite of both summer and ice anglers — they're easy to catch, willing biters, and excellent eating. They are also a hugely important forage fish for larger predators.

## What does a yellow perch eat?

Yellow perch shift diet as they grow. Young-of-the-year eat zooplankton; as they grow they switch to bottom invertebrates (insects, larvae, crustaceans like Gammarus); and larger perch add small fish, fish eggs, and minnows. They feed actively during daylight, often near structure and vegetation.

## When is a yellow perch in season?

Yellow perch are caught year-round and are an especially popular ice-fishing target in winter and a reliable warm-season panfish. They're typically managed under general panfish rules, though some waters have specific limits, set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a yellow perch spawn?

Yellow perch spawn in spring (generally February–July in the north, depending on latitude and water temperature), shortly after ice-out in northern lakes. The female releases eggs in a long, distinctive gelatinous, accordion-folded ribbon that drapes over vegetation, brush, and structure — there is no nest and no parental care. Eggs hatch in roughly 8–12 days depending on temperature. Perch produce large numbers of eggs to offset heavy predation on their young.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Yellow perch live in freshwater ponds, lakes, and slow rivers (tolerating some brackish water at the edges of their range).

## Fun fact / angler note

Yellow perch lay their eggs in unmistakable long, lacy, accordion-folded ribbons draped over weeds and branches — unlike almost any other freshwater fish. They school tightly, so when you catch one, more are usually nearby — a big reason they're a favorite for kids and ice anglers. And as the primary prey of walleye, healthy perch numbers often mean healthy walleye fishing.

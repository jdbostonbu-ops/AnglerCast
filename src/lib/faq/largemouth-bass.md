# Largemouth Bass (Micropterus salmoides)

Also known as: largemouth, bigmouth, bucketmouth, black bass, green bass.

## What is a largemouth bass?

The largemouth bass is the most popular freshwater gamefish in North America — a streamlined, hard-striking member of the sunfish family with a very large mouth whose upper jaw extends past the eye. It is greenish above with a broad dark stripe along the midside. Largemouth thrive in warm, weedy, slow or still waters: ponds, lakes, reservoirs, and quiet backwaters of rivers. They relate to cover — weeds, docks, timber, and structure — and are most active at dawn and dusk. Their explosive strikes and willingness to hit a huge variety of lures make them the centerpiece of recreational and tournament bass fishing.

## What does a largemouth bass eat?

Largemouth bass are voracious, opportunistic predators. Juveniles eat small insects, zooplankton, and other small invertebrates; adults are strongly carnivorous, feeding on fish, crayfish, large insects, frogs, and occasionally mice, snakes, or other small animals that fall into the water. They are ambush predators that use cover to surprise prey, which is why bass anglers fish around weeds, wood, and structure.

## When is a largemouth bass in season?

Largemouth are a warm-water fish caught spring through fall, with pre-spawn spring and fall feeding periods especially productive; they slow down but can still be caught in winter from deeper water. Seasons, size limits, and bag limits (and special catch-and-release periods during the spawn in some states) are set by state fish and wildlife agencies and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a largemouth bass spawn?

Largemouth spawn in spring to early summer, when water warms to around 60°F (in the Northeast, generally late spring into June). The male sweeps out a shallow nest, usually in 1–4 feet of water over firm bottom, and aggressively guards the eggs and fry. Females may drop multiple batches of eggs across the season. The guarding male is what makes bedding bass both catchable and vulnerable, which is why some states protect them during the spawn.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Largemouth bass live in warm freshwater lakes, ponds, reservoirs, and slow rivers.

## Fun fact / angler note

The largemouth's nickname "bucketmouth" is well earned — its mouth is big enough to engulf surprisingly large prey, and it'll attack everything from plastic worms to topwater frogs to live shiners. It's the №1 target of the entire bass-fishing industry, and a calm dawn topwater strike from a big largemouth is one of freshwater fishing's signature thrills.

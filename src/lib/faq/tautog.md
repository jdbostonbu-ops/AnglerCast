# Tautog (Tautoga onitis)

Also known as: blackfish, tog, white chin.

## What is a tautog?

The tautog, almost universally called blackfish, is a stocky, dark-colored member of the wrasse family found along the U.S. Atlantic coast from Nova Scotia to South Carolina, most common from Cape Cod to the Mid-Atlantic. It has a blunt head, thick rubbery lips, and powerful crushing teeth. Tautog are structure-oriented fish that live around rocky bottoms, reefs, wrecks, jetties, pilings, and mussel beds. They grow slowly, are long-lived, and a trophy "tog" can exceed 20 pounds.

## What does a tautog eat?

Tautog are specialized hard-shell feeders. Their blunt, conical teeth are built for crushing, and their diet is dominated by mussels, barnacles, crabs, snails, clams, shrimp, and small lobsters. Green crabs and other crabs are classic tautog bait because they match the fish's natural diet around structure.

## When is a tautog in season?

Tautog are targeted in the Northeast mainly in spring and again in fall, when they hold on nearshore structure; many states restrict or close the harvest in mid-summer. Seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and individual states and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a tautog spawn?

Tautog spawn in spring and early summer, generally moving from deeper water into shallower nearshore reefs and rocky areas to do so (roughly April–June in the north). The eggs are pelagic and drift in the water column. After spawning, fish disperse, and as waters cool in fall they feed aggressively on structure before larger fish move to deeper water for winter. Tautog grow slowly and can live several decades.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Tautog are a marine, structure-associated species living over rocky bottoms, reefs, and wrecks in coastal ocean waters.

## Fun fact / angler note

Tautog are famous for "taste-testing" a bait before committing, which makes the bite tricky to detect and earns them a reputation as a challenging, skill-rewarding fish. They hold tight to rock and structure, so anglers fish bottom rigs with crab or clam right in the rocks — and break-offs are part of the game.

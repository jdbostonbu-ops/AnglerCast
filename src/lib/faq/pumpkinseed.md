# Pumpkinseed (Lepomis gibbosus)

Also known as: pumpkinseed sunfish, sunny, common sunfish, punkie, kivver.

## What is a pumpkinseed?

The pumpkinseed is one of the most beautiful and common small sunfish, named for its round, flat, seed-shaped body. It's brilliantly colored — olive and gold flecked with orange, blue, and green, with wavy bright-blue lines on the cheeks and gill covers and a distinctive bright orange or red spot at the tip of the black ear flap (a key way to tell it from a bluegill). Pumpkinseeds live in weedy, shallow areas of warm ponds, lakes, and slow streams, often right alongside bluegill. They rarely exceed 6–8 inches, are easy to catch, and are a beloved first fish for young anglers.

## What does a pumpkinseed eat?

Pumpkinseeds eat aquatic insects, larvae, small crustaceans, worms, and — notably — snails and small mollusks, which they crush with specially adapted teeth in their throat. They forage in and around weed beds and cover, browsing vegetation for snails and picking small invertebrates from the bottom and plants.

## When is a pumpkinseed in season?

Pumpkinseeds are caught year-round and are a reliable, willing warm-season panfish (and ice-fishing catch). They're usually managed under general panfish/sunfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a pumpkinseed spawn?

Pumpkinseeds spawn from late spring into summer (beginning around 68°F), nesting in colonies much like bluegill — and sometimes building their nests right in bluegill colonies. The male fans out a nest in the shallows, the female deposits eggs, and the male guards the eggs and fry. They're prolific spawners, which keeps them abundant in suitable ponds and lakes.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Pumpkinseeds live in weedy, warm freshwater ponds, lakes, and slow streams.

## Fun fact / angler note

The orange-red dot on the black ear flap is the quick, reliable way to separate a pumpkinseed from a bluegill. Pumpkinseeds are among the most colorful freshwater fish in North America, especially breeding males with their electric-blue cheek lines — and they bite so readily on a worm under a bobber that they're a perfect fish for getting kids hooked on fishing.

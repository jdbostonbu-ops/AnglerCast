# White Hake (Urophycis tenuis)

Also known as: hake, mud hake, sow hake, russet hake, blue hake.

## What is a white hake?

The white hake is a soft-bodied bottom-dwelling fish in the phycid hake group, found in the North Atlantic from the Gulf of St. Lawrence and Newfoundland south along the U.S. shelf, common in the Gulf of Maine. It has an elongated body, a long second dorsal and anal fin, a single chin barbel, and threadlike pelvic fins it uses to feel along the bottom. White hake live on soft mud bottoms, often in deeper water, and are a significant commercial groundfish and an incidental rod-and-reel catch.

## What does a white hake eat?

White hake are bottom predators. They feed on fish, shrimp, krill, squid, crabs, and other crustaceans, using their sensitive pelvic-fin feelers and chin barbel to locate prey on and near the muddy bottom. Larger white hake become more strongly fish-eating.

## When is a white hake in season?

White hake are present in the Gulf of Maine and New England waters year-round in suitable depths, typically in deeper water. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and the New England Fishery Management Council, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a white hake spawn?

White hake are broadcast spawners that release pelagic eggs into the water column, generally spawning in late winter and spring in deeper offshore waters. Eggs and larvae drift in the water column; uniquely, juvenile white hake often settle first into shallow seagrass and inshore habitats before moving to deeper mud bottoms as they grow.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. White hake are a marine groundfish, living mostly on soft, deep mud bottoms in the Gulf of Maine and along the continental shelf.

## Fun fact / angler note

White hake have soft, delicate flesh and a slightly different reputation than their relatives — they feel along the bottom with thread-like pelvic fins like a pair of antennae. They're often caught incidentally by anglers fishing deep for cod and haddock, and they need quick icing because the flesh softens fast.

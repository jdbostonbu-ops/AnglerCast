# Redbreast Sunfish (Lepomis auritus)

Also known as: redbreast, robin, sun perch, yellowbelly sunfish, river sunfish.

## What is a redbreast sunfish?

The redbreast sunfish is a colorful native sunfish of the Atlantic slope, found in streams, rivers, and ponds along the East Coast and into the Northeast. It resembles a bluegill but is more elongated, with a bright orange-to-red breast (especially on males) and a long, narrow, all-black ear flap without a pale border. Redbreasts are especially associated with flowing water — they're a characteristic sunfish of rivers and streams as well as ponds — and they're a scrappy, willing panfish that anglers enjoy on light tackle.

## What does a redbreast sunfish eat?

Redbreast sunfish eat aquatic and terrestrial insects, larvae, small crustaceans, snails, worms, and small fish. In rivers they pick insects and invertebrates from the bottom and from drifting current, and larger fish take small minnows and crayfish.

## When is a redbreast sunfish in season?

Redbreast sunfish are caught spring through fall and are a willing warm-season panfish, especially in rivers and streams. They're usually managed under general panfish/sunfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a redbreast sunfish spawn?

Redbreast sunfish spawn in late spring and summer, when the male fans out a nest in the shallows (often near cover or in slower stream margins) and guards the eggs and fry, like other sunfish. They sometimes nest near or among other sunfish species, and hybridization with bluegill and other Lepomis can occur.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Redbreast sunfish live in freshwater rivers, streams, and ponds — they're especially at home in flowing water.

## Fun fact / angler note

The redbreast's long, narrow, solid-black ear flap (with no pale margin) plus its bright orange-red belly make it easy to tell from a bluegill. Because they thrive in moving water, redbreasts are a favorite target of stream and river anglers fly-fishing or float-fishing for panfish, and breeding males in full color are striking.

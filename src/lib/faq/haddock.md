# Haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus)

Also known as: scrod haddock (small), finnan haddie (smoked).

## What is a haddock?

The haddock is a North Atlantic groundfish in the cod family, found on both sides of the ocean; in the northwest Atlantic it ranges from New Jersey to Newfoundland, with two U.S. stocks on Georges Bank and in the Gulf of Maine. It resembles cod but is distinguished by a dark "thumbprint" spot above the pectoral fin and a black lateral line (cod's is pale). Haddock live near the bottom over gravel, sand, and rocky substrate and are a major commercial and recreational groundfish in New England.

## What does a haddock eat?

Haddock are bottom feeders with a notable taste for invertebrates. Echinoderms — brittle stars, sea urchins, and sand dollars — are a consistent part of their diet across regions, along with worms, small crustaceans, mollusks, and fish eggs. Unlike cod, haddock don't become as strongly fish-eating as they grow, though they do take some small fish.

## When is a haddock in season?

Haddock are a cold-water groundfish present in New England waters year-round in suitable depths, with seasonal inshore-offshore movement. The fishery is managed by the New England Fishery Management Council and NOAA Fisheries, with seasons and limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a haddock spawn?

Haddock are broadcast spawners that release pelagic (drifting) eggs into the water column over traditional spawning grounds, generally in late winter and spring. Larger, older females tend to spawn earlier in the season, and the spawning season stretches over roughly three months. Eggs and larvae drift with currents before the young settle to the bottom.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Haddock are a marine groundfish of the cold North Atlantic, living near the bottom on offshore banks and in the Gulf of Maine.

## Fun fact / angler note

The dark spot above a haddock's pectoral fin is sometimes called "the Devil's thumbprint" in old fishermen's lore. Haddock is prized at the table — it's the classic fish for smoked "finnan haddie" and a New England fish-and-chips staple. The black lateral line (versus cod's white) is the quick way to tell the two apart at the rail.

# Atlantic Herring (Clupea harengus)

Also known as: herring, sea herring, sild.

## What is an Atlantic herring?

The Atlantic herring is a small, silvery, schooling pelagic fish and one of the most abundant fish in the cool northern Atlantic. In the western Atlantic it ranges along the continental shelf from Labrador to Cape Hatteras, separated by biologists into Gulf of Maine and Georges Bank stocks. Usually under a foot long, herring travel in enormous schools in open water and are a cornerstone forage fish — food for cod, striped bass, bluefish, tuna, whales, seals, and seabirds. They are also a major commercial species and a key bait fish.

## What does an Atlantic herring eat?

Atlantic herring are filter and particle feeders that eat plankton. Their diet is dominated by copepods and other zooplankton, along with small crustaceans, fish eggs, and fish larvae. They feed in the water column as they move with their schools.

## When is an Atlantic herring in season?

Herring are present in the Gulf of Maine and New England waters and move seasonally; they are most accessible inshore at certain times of year depending on the stock. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and regional councils, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an Atlantic herring spawn?

Atlantic herring are unusual in that they spawn demersal (bottom-stuck) eggs rather than drifting eggs — females deposit eggs that adhere to the bottom, gravel, and vegetation. Different groups spawn at different times, but western Atlantic herring largely spawn in late summer and fall on offshore banks and ledges. (Note: the similarly named blueback herring and alewife are different, anadromous river-herring species that spawn in spring in fresh water.) Atlantic herring reach maturity around age 4.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Atlantic herring are a pelagic marine species of the open continental shelf and coastal waters — they live and spawn in salt water, unlike the anadromous "river herrings."

## Fun fact / angler note

Atlantic herring schools can be staggering in size and are a major reason gamefish show up where they do — find the herring and you often find the striped bass, bluefish, cod, and tuna feeding on them. Herring are also a top bait, used whole or cut for many larger species.

# Rainbow Smelt (Osmerus mordax)

Also known as: smelt, American smelt, freshwater smelt, frostfish.

## What is a rainbow smelt?

The rainbow smelt is a small, slender, silvery fish with a faint iridescent purple, blue, and pink sheen along its sides and a distinctive cucumber-like smell when fresh. It is anadromous — living in coastal salt water and running up freshwater streams to spawn — and is found along the northern Atlantic coast and in cold northern lakes. There are also fully landlocked populations that live their entire lives in fresh water (including in many northern lakes). Smelt run in large numbers and are a traditional cold-weather catch, often taken through the ice or with dip nets during spawning runs.

## What does a rainbow smelt eat?

Rainbow smelt are predators on plankton and small prey. They eat zooplankton (like copepods and small crustaceans), insect larvae, worms, and small fish, including the young of other fish. Larger smelt become more fish-eating. They are themselves a key forage fish for many larger predators, including trout, salmon, striped bass, and lake trout.

## When is a rainbow smelt in season?

Smelt are best known as a cold-season fish — they are traditionally targeted in late fall through winter and during their early-spring spawning runs, including by ice fishing and dip-netting where allowed. Seasons and methods are regulated by state agencies and vary widely. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a rainbow smelt spawn?

Rainbow smelt spawn in early spring, running up freshwater streams and rivers (or to the shallows of lakes for landlocked populations) shortly after ice-out, typically at night. The eggs are sticky and adhere to gravel, rocks, and vegetation. After spawning, adults return downstream to salt water (or deeper lake water), and the young drift down to grow.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both. Rainbow smelt are anadromous — they live in coastal salt water and spawn in fresh water. There are also landlocked freshwater populations that complete their entire life cycle in lakes, which is why smelt appear in both saltwater and freshwater settings.

## Fun fact / angler note

Fresh rainbow smelt famously smell like cut cucumber — it's a real identification clue. They're a beloved cold-weather catch, often fried whole and eaten bones and all, and a classic ice-fishing and dip-net target during the spring run. As forage, smelt are a major food source for trophy lake trout and landlocked salmon.

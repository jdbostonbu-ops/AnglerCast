# Green Sunfish (Lepomis cyanellus)

Also known as: green sunfish, green perch, pond perch, rubbertail, sand bass.

## What is a green sunfish?

The green sunfish is a hardy, aggressive little sunfish with a more bass-like body and a noticeably larger mouth than most of its relatives. It is greenish with a yellow belly, scattered blue-green flecks, wavy blue lines on the cheeks, and bright orange or yellow edges on the fins, with a black ear flap. Green sunfish are extremely tolerant of poor conditions — low oxygen, turbid water, small pools — and can live where few other fish do, which makes them common in creeks, ponds, ditches, and small reservoirs. Because of their tolerance and aggression, they can overpopulate and stunt in small waters.

## What does a green sunfish eat?

Green sunfish are more predatory than bluegill or redear thanks to their larger mouth. They eat aquatic and terrestrial insects, crustaceans, crayfish, snails, worms, and small fish, and will readily take other fish's young. This aggressive, fish-eating habit means they compete directly with young gamefish.

## When is a green sunfish in season?

Green sunfish are caught spring through fall and are aggressive, easy biters. They're usually managed under general panfish/sunfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change, and in some waters managers encourage harvesting them to prevent overcrowding. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a green sunfish spawn?

Green sunfish spawn from late spring into summer, with the male fanning out a nest in the shallows and guarding the eggs and fry, like other sunfish. They spawn readily and prolifically — combined with their hardiness, this is why they so often overpopulate small ponds and become stunted.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Green sunfish live in freshwater creeks, ponds, ditches, and small reservoirs, tolerating conditions many other fish can't.

## Fun fact / angler note

The green sunfish's larger, more bass-like mouth is the tell — it'll attack lures and baits far bigger than a bluegill would, which makes it a surprisingly aggressive little fighter. Their toughness is legendary: they often turn up in marginal waters, farm ponds, and small creeks where almost nothing else survives.

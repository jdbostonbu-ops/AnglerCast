# Atlantic Croaker (Micropogonias undulatus)

Also known as: croaker, hardhead.

## What is an Atlantic croaker?

The Atlantic croaker is a coastal member of the drum family (Sciaenidae), named for the croaking or drumming sound it makes using muscles against its swim bladder. It ranges along the U.S. Atlantic coast from around Massachusetts (where it is uncommon and at the northern edge of its range) south to the Gulf of Mexico, and is most abundant in the Mid-Atlantic and South. Croaker are silvery with a coppery or pinkish tint and faint wavy bars, and they live over mud, sand, and shell bottoms in estuaries, bays, and nearshore waters.

## What does an Atlantic croaker eat?

Atlantic croaker are bottom-dwelling predators with broad, opportunistic diets. They feed on a wide range of bottom invertebrates — amphipods, worms (polychaetes), shrimp, small crabs (including blue crabs), mollusks — along with small fish and detritus. Their varied diet lets them adapt to whatever is locally abundant.

## When is an Atlantic croaker in season?

Croaker are a warm-season inshore presence in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic, with abundance in the region varying year to year (they are at the edge of their range in southern New England). Seasons and limits, where they apply, are set by state agencies and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and can change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an Atlantic croaker spawn?

Atlantic croaker spawn offshore in coastal waters, primarily in fall and into winter, as adults move out of the estuaries. The eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift toward shore, and the young use coastal and estuarine waters as nursery grounds, where they feed and grow before joining the coastal population.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Atlantic croaker are a coastal marine and estuarine species (euryhaline — tolerant of a range of salinities); they use estuaries as nurseries but spawn in the ocean and live in salt and brackish water.

## Fun fact / angler note

Croaker really do croak — pick one up and you may hear it drum, a sound produced by muscles vibrating against the swim bladder, used in courtship and as a social signal. They're a popular, scrappy panfish and good eating, often caught on simple bottom rigs with shrimp or worm.

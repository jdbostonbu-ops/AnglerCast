# Bluegill (Lepomis macrochirus)

Also known as: bream, brim, sunny, copperbelly, panfish.

## What is a bluegill?

The bluegill is the classic American sunfish — the panfish most people picture and the one many anglers first caught as kids. It is a deep, flat, plate-shaped fish with a small mouth, a dark spot at the rear of the dorsal fin, and a black "ear" flap, often with a coppery-orange breast on breeding males. Bluegill live in warm ponds, lakes, and slow streams across nearly the entire country, schooling around weeds, docks, and cover. They're abundant, eager biters, and excellent eating, and they're the backbone of the food web that supports largemouth bass.

## What does a bluegill eat?

Bluegill have small mouths and feed mostly on small prey: aquatic and terrestrial insects, insect larvae, zooplankton, small crustaceans, snails, fish eggs, and small fish, plus some aquatic vegetation when other food is scarce. Their small mouth means they take small baits — worms, crickets, and tiny jigs and flies.

## When is a bluegill in season?

Bluegill are caught year-round, with peak action around their long late-spring-to-summer spawn, and they're a popular ice-fishing target too. They're usually managed under general panfish/sunfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a bluegill spawn?

Bluegill are colony nesters. From late spring through much of the summer, in water around 67–80°F, males fan out saucer-shaped nests packed close together in firm-bottomed shallows, creating distinctive "beds" of dozens of nests. A single female can drop tens of thousands of eggs, and the male guards the eggs and fry. In warm regions bluegill may spawn several times a season, which is why they can become very abundant.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Bluegill live in warm freshwater ponds, lakes, and slow streams.

## Fun fact / angler note

When bluegill are on the beds in early summer, you can sometimes see the clustered, crater-like nests in clear shallows — and the guarding males will smack almost any small bait or fly that drifts over them. Because bluegill are the primary forage that grows big largemouth bass, a healthy bluegill population is the foundation of good bass fishing. They're also the quintessential kid's first fish: willing, abundant, and tasty.

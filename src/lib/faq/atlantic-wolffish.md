# Atlantic Wolffish (Anarhichas lupus)

Also known as: seawolf, ocean catfish, Atlantic catfish, devil fish, sea cat, woof.

## What is an Atlantic wolffish?

The Atlantic wolffish is a striking, large-headed, eel-bodied bottom fish of cold North Atlantic waters, found in the Gulf of Maine and northward over rocky, hard bottoms. It has a blunt face and a fearsome mouth full of strong canine and crushing teeth, used to feed on hard-shelled prey. Despite the intimidating look, it is a slow-moving, structure-bound fish, not aggressive toward people. Atlantic wolffish are slow-growing and have declined sharply in U.S. waters; they are designated a NOAA Species of Concern, and there are restrictions on keeping them.

## What does an Atlantic wolffish eat?

Atlantic wolffish are specialized hard-shell crushers. Their powerful teeth let them feed on mollusks, crabs, sea urchins, whelks, large snails, hermit crabs, and other hard-shelled and spiny invertebrates. They use their strong jaws to crush shells that most fish can't handle, playing an important role in controlling sea urchin and green crab populations.

## When is an Atlantic wolffish in season?

In U.S. Atlantic waters, the Atlantic wolffish is a Species of Concern and its harvest is prohibited in federal waters, so anglers who catch one are generally required to release it. Regulations are set by NOAA Fisheries and state agencies and can change. Always check current regulations — in most cases this fish must be released.

## When does an Atlantic wolffish spawn?

Unusually for a cold-water fish, Atlantic wolffish use internal fertilization and lay large eggs in nests on the bottom, which the male guards. Spawning generally occurs in fall and winter. The eggs are large and few in number compared to broadcast spawners, and the guarded nest and slow growth are part of why the species is slow to recover from population declines.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Atlantic wolffish are a cold-water marine species of rocky, hard bottoms in the Gulf of Maine and the North Atlantic.

## Fun fact / angler note

Atlantic wolffish produce a natural antifreeze in their blood to survive in near-freezing water, and they're one of the few fish that guard their eggs in a nest. Because they're a Species of Concern with prohibited harvest in U.S. federal waters, the right thing to do if you catch one is to release it carefully — and admire that remarkable toothy face on the way back.

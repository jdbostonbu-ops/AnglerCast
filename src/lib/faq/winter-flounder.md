# Winter Flounder (Pseudopleuronectes americanus)

Also known as: blackback, lemon sole, Georges Bank flounder.

## What is a winter flounder?

The winter flounder is a right-eyed flatfish (both eyes on the right side) native to the Northwest Atlantic, common in coastal New England and Mid-Atlantic estuaries, bays, and nearshore waters. It is a small-mouthed bottom-dweller and a cold-tolerant species — it stays in shallow inshore waters through the colder months when many other flatfish have moved offshore, which is the origin of its name. It is an important inshore recreational and commercial fish in the Northeast.

## What does a winter flounder eat?

Winter flounder have small mouths and feed on small bottom invertebrates. Their diet includes polychaete worms, amphipods, small crustaceans, copepods (in younger fish), and the siphons of clams such as soft-shell clams (Mya arenaria). Diet shifts as they grow — the smallest fish eat tiny copepods and worms, while larger fish take a wider range of bottom invertebrates.

## When is a winter flounder in season?

Winter flounder are an inshore presence in the colder months and early spring in the Northeast, staying in shallow bays and estuaries when many other species have left. Recreational seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and individual states and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a winter flounder spawn?

Winter flounder spawn in shallow water inside estuaries, bays, coves, and river mouths. Unlike most fish in this list, their eggs are demersal — they sink and stick to the bottom rather than drifting. Spawning occurs in winter in the southern part of the range and in spring farther north, generally peaking around February–March, with eggs hatching in about 15–18 days. Adults show strong fidelity, tending to return to the same spawning estuary each year. The estuary's protected, low-current upper reaches help retain the eggs and larvae.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Winter flounder live in coastal salt water — estuaries, bays, salt ponds, and nearshore ocean. They depend on estuaries for spawning and as nursery habitat, but the species is marine.

## Fun fact / angler note

Winter flounder are sometimes called "blackbacks," and their cold tolerance is remarkable — they remain active and feeding in water cold enough to drive most flatfish offshore, and their larvae can withstand short-term cold shock. Their small mouths mean anglers typically use small hooks and bait when targeting them.

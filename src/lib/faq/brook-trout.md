# Brook Trout (Salvelinus fontinalis)

Also known as: brookie, speckled trout, squaretail, native trout, char.

## What is a brook trout?

The brook trout is a beautiful, cold-water fish native to eastern North America, from Georgia north to Québec. Despite the name, it is technically a char (like lake trout), recognizable by its dark green, marbled "vermiculated" back, red spots ringed with blue halos, and white-edged lower fins. Brook trout need cold, clean, well-oxygenated water and are a treasured native species in headwater streams, spring-fed creeks, and cold ponds — often considered an indicator of healthy, pristine water. In the Northeast they're a prized wild and stocked gamefish.

## What does a brook trout eat?

Brook trout are opportunistic feeders. They eat aquatic and terrestrial insects (mayflies, caddisflies, stoneflies, midges, and beetles and ants that fall in), larvae, crustaceans, worms, small fish, and fish eggs. Larger brook trout become more fish-eating. Their willingness to take drifting insects is what makes them a classic fly-fishing target.

## When is a brook trout in season?

Brook trout are a cold-water species most active in cooler conditions; in the Northeast they're targeted heavily in spring and fall, and they seek cold spring-fed refuges in summer heat. Seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by state fish and wildlife agencies and change from year to year, with special rules on many wild-trout waters. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a brook trout spawn?

Brook trout spawn in fall, typically October–November, when the female digs a nest ("redd") in clean gravel, often over upwelling groundwater or spring seeps that supply cold, oxygenated water. The eggs incubate over winter and hatch in early spring. Reliable cold, clean, oxygen-rich water is essential for the eggs to survive, which is why brook trout are so sensitive to warming and pollution.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Brook trout live in cold freshwater streams, spring creeks, headwaters, and ponds. (In some coastal areas a sea-run form called the "salter" ventures briefly into salt water, but the species is fundamentally a freshwater char.)

## Fun fact / angler note

Brook trout aren't true trout at all — they're char, related to lake trout and Arctic char. They're also the state fish of several eastern states and a living symbol of clean, cold water: where wild brook trout thrive, the watershed is usually in good shape. A wild "brookie" in full fall spawning color is one of the most beautiful fish in fresh water.

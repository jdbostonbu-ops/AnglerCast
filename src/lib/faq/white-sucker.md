# White Sucker (Catostomus commersonii)

Also known as: sucker, common sucker, mud sucker, brook sucker, black sucker.

## What is a white sucker?

The white sucker is an abundant, widespread native bottom-feeding fish, recognizable by its rounded, downward-facing, fleshy "sucker" mouth used to vacuum food off the bottom. It has a long, cylindrical body, ranging from olive-brown to gray above and pale below (breeding males develop a darker stripe and tubercles). White suckers live in a wide range of fresh waters — streams, rivers, ponds, and lakes — and tolerate varied conditions. They're a key part of the food web (forage for pike, bass, walleye, and other predators) and an important bait and even food fish, with large spring spawning runs up tributary streams.

## What does a white sucker eat?

White suckers are bottom feeders that use their protrusible, downward-pointing mouth to suck up food from the bottom. Their diet is made up of aquatic insect larvae, small crustaceans, worms, mollusks, algae, plant material, and detritus. They root and vacuum along the bottom, processing sediment for small organisms.

## When is a white sucker in season?

White suckers are caught spring through fall, with the spring spawning run a traditional time to catch them (including for bait and for eating). They're usually managed as a nongame or bait species under general rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations.

## When does a white sucker spawn?

White suckers make spring spawning runs, ascending tributary streams and rivers (or moving to lake-shore shallows) in large numbers, typically when water warms in spring. They broadcast their eggs over gravel riffles — there is no nest and no parental care — and the sticky eggs settle into the gravel. These spring runs concentrate fish and are an important food pulse for predators and, historically, for people.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. White suckers live in freshwater streams, rivers, ponds, and lakes.

## Fun fact / angler note

Suckers get an undeserved bad rap — that downturned, rubbery mouth is a perfectly engineered bottom-vacuum, and white suckers are a crucial forage fish that helps grow trophy pike, bass, and walleye. Their spring runs up small streams are a classic event, and they're widely used as bait (and eaten, often smoked). Young suckers are also a favorite live bait for big predators.

# Monkfish (Lophius americanus)

Also known as: goosefish, anglerfish, allmouth, fishtail, poor man's lobster.

## What is a monkfish?

The monkfish, officially the goosefish, is a strange-looking ambush predator that lies camouflaged on the bottom. It has an enormous flattened head, a huge upward-facing mouth full of long teeth, and a body that tapers to a relatively small tail. A modified first dorsal spine on its head acts as a built-in fishing lure (the "illicium"), which it wiggles to draw prey toward its mouth. Monkfish range along the U.S. and Canadian Atlantic shelf from the Grand Banks to North Carolina, from shallow coastal waters to deep offshore. They are a valuable commercial species, prized for their firm, sweet tail meat.

## What does a monkfish eat?

Monkfish are voracious sit-and-wait ambush predators with enormous mouths, and they eat almost anything they can engulf. Their diet is dominated by fish — including flatfish, herring, dogfish, skates, and other monkfish — but they also take crustaceans, squid, and even diving seabirds. They lure prey with the wiggling lure on their head, then engulf it whole with a sudden gulp.

## When is a monkfish in season?

Monkfish are present along the New England and Mid-Atlantic shelf year-round in suitable depths, moving seasonally between shallow and deep water. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and regional councils, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a monkfish spawn?

Monkfish spawning is remarkable: females release their eggs in a single enormous, buoyant, ribbon-like mucous "egg veil" that can be many feet long and contain over a million eggs, floating at the surface. Spawning generally occurs in spring and summer, with timing varying by area. The larvae are pelagic and drift before settling to the bottom.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Monkfish are a marine bottom-dwelling species of the continental shelf, from shallow coastal waters to deep offshore grounds.

## Fun fact / angler note

Monkfish are sometimes called "poor man's lobster" because the firm, sweet tail meat is reminiscent of lobster. The buoyant egg veil — a floating ribbon holding over a million eggs — is one of the most unusual reproductive structures of any fish. Anglers who hook one are always struck by the giant mouth and the little built-in "fishing rod" on its head.

# Mahi-Mahi (Coryphaena hippurus)

Also known as: dolphinfish, dolphin, dorado.

## What is a mahi-mahi?

The mahi-mahi, also called dolphinfish or dorado, is a stunning, fast-growing pelagic fish famous for brilliant colors — electric blues and greens with golden-yellow flanks that flash and shift, fading quickly after the fish is landed. Males ("bulls") develop a tall, blunt forehead, while females have a rounded head. Mahi are a warm-water species found in offshore waters worldwide, reaching the Northeast offshore grounds in the warmer months, often around weed lines and floating debris. They are one of the fastest-growing fish in the ocean and a favorite offshore sport and table fish.

## What does a mahi-mahi eat?

Mahi-mahi are fast, aggressive surface predators. They feed on small fish such as flying fish and small mackerels, along with squid, crustaceans, and the small creatures that gather around floating objects and weed lines. They hunt in fast bursts near the surface and often travel in schools.

## When is a mahi-mahi in season?

Mahi appear off the Northeast seasonally in the warmer months, when warm water and weed lines push up onto offshore grounds. They are managed by NOAA Fisheries and regional councils, with limits that can change from year to year. Always check current regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a mahi-mahi spawn?

Mahi-mahi are batch spawners that release pelagic eggs into warm open water, and they spawn frequently — often multiple times per season in warm conditions. They mature very young (within their first year) and grow extremely fast, which is part of why the species is relatively resilient. Eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift in warm offshore water.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Mahi-mahi are a warm-water open-ocean (pelagic) marine species, often found near the surface around weed lines and floating structure.

## Fun fact / angler note

Despite the name "dolphinfish," mahi-mahi are true fish, unrelated to the marine mammal — the name "mahi-mahi" caught on partly to avoid confusion at the seafood counter. They're among the fastest-growing fish known and put on dazzling color displays when hooked. Anglers find them around floating debris and weed lines, where they school and feed aggressively.

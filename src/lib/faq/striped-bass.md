# Striped Bass (Morone saxatilis)

Also known as: striper, linesider, rock, rockfish.

## What is a striped bass?

The striped bass is one of the most prized gamefish on the U.S. Atlantic coast, recognized by the dark horizontal stripes running along its silvery sides. It is an anadromous fish — it lives in salt water but migrates into fresh or brackish rivers to spawn. Striped bass range from the St. Lawrence River in Canada to the St. Johns River in Florida, and have also been widely introduced into inland lakes and reservoirs as a landlocked freshwater sportfish. They are large, long-lived fish: 50- to 60-pound fish are not unheard of, and they can live up to about 30 years.

## What does a striped bass eat?

Striped bass are opportunistic predators. Adults feed heavily on schooling baitfish such as menhaden (bunker), herring, alewife, and silversides, and also take squid, crabs, lobsters, worms, and other crustaceans. In inland and landlocked populations, shad are often a major food source. They feed actively at dawn, dusk, and night, and follow bait schools closely.

## When is a striped bass in season?

Along the Northeast coast, striped bass are a spring-through-fall inshore presence, moving north and inshore as waters warm and returning south or offshore as they cool. Striped bass are tightly regulated, with slot limits, seasons, and bag limits set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and individual states, and these change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping a fish.

## When does a striped bass spawn?

Striped bass are anadromous and ascend coastal rivers to spawn in fresh or brackish water, typically from April to June, with peak activity when water temperatures are roughly 59–68°F (15–20°C). Major spawning populations occur in Chesapeake Bay, the Hudson River, and the Delaware, Roanoke, and Kennebec river systems. Females broadcast eggs near the surface in moving water (current is needed to keep the eggs suspended). After spawning, females return to the ocean while males may linger in the estuary. Juveniles remain in estuaries and river deltas for 2 to 4 years before joining the coastal migratory population. Males mature around age 2, females around age 6.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both. Striped bass are naturally anadromous — they live in coastal salt water but spawn in fresh or brackish rivers. There are also fully landlocked freshwater populations stocked in large reservoirs and impoundments, where they live their whole lives in fresh water.

## Fun fact / angler note

Striped bass overwintering in fresh water versus the ocean can even differ in color — fish that hold in rivers tend to look darker, while ocean fish appear greener. After a serious decline in the 1970s–80s, coordinated interstate management helped striped bass stocks rebound dramatically by the late 1990s, making them one of the best-known fishery recovery stories on the Atlantic coast.

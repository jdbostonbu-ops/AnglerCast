# Lake Trout (Salvelinus namaycush)

Also known as: laker, togue, mackinaw, gray trout, char.

## What is a lake trout?

The lake trout is the largest char in North America, a deep, cold-water fish of large northern lakes, including waters like Lake Champlain. Like the brook trout, it is technically a char, not a true trout. It is gray to greenish with profuse pale spots and a deeply forked tail, and it grows slowly to a large size, living for decades. Lake trout demand cold, well-oxygenated, deep water and retreat to the depths in summer, which is why they're often caught by deep trolling or jigging. They are a prized trophy and table fish in northern lakes.

## What does a lake trout eat?

Lake trout are predators whose diet is dominated by other fish. In many lakes, rainbow smelt and ciscoes (coregonines) are primary prey; they also eat other small fish, insects, and crustaceans, especially when young. Larger lake trout are strongly fish-eating, following schools of forage fish through the cold depths.

## When is a lake trout in season?

Lake trout are a deep, cold-water species accessible near the surface in cold months (including through the ice) and found deep during summer. Seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by state fish and wildlife agencies and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a lake trout spawn?

Lake trout spawn in fall, generally October–November, over rocky reefs and rubble in lakes — uniquely, they do not dig a redd. Instead, they broadcast their eggs over clean rocky bottom, and the eggs fall into the crevices to incubate over winter, hatching in early spring. They are slow-growing and late-maturing, which makes lake trout populations vulnerable to overharvest.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Lake trout are a strictly freshwater char of cold, deep northern lakes.

## Fun fact / angler note

Lake trout are among the longest-lived freshwater fish — in cold northern lakes they can live well over 20 years, and some far older. They're unusual among trout and char for broadcasting eggs over rocky reefs rather than digging a nest. In summer, anglers go deep for them with downriggers and heavy jigs, since the fish follow the cold water far below the surface.

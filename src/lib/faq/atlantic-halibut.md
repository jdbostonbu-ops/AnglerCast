# Atlantic Halibut (Hippoglossus hippoglossus)

Also known as: halibut, giant halibut.

## What is an Atlantic halibut?

The Atlantic halibut is the largest flatfish in the world — a powerful right-eyed flatfish of the cold North Atlantic that can reach several hundred pounds and live for decades. It ranges from the Gulf of Maine and Georges Bank northward, living on sand, gravel, and clay bottoms in deep, cold water. Unlike the small flatfish anglers usually encounter, a big halibut is a formidable predator. Atlantic halibut were historically overfished and remain at low abundance in U.S. waters, with strict harvest limits.

## What does an Atlantic halibut eat?

Atlantic halibut are strong, active predators — much more piscivorous than most flatfish. They feed heavily on fish such as cod, haddock, herring, sand lance, redfish, and other flatfish, along with squid, crabs, lobsters, and other large invertebrates. Big halibut will chase prey well up off the bottom.

## When is an Atlantic halibut in season?

Atlantic halibut are a deepwater coldwater groundfish present in the Gulf of Maine year-round in suitable depths. They are tightly regulated due to low abundance, with very restrictive limits set by NOAA Fisheries and state agencies (for example, a small daily limit in some areas). Always check current regulations before keeping one.

## When does an Atlantic halibut spawn?

Atlantic halibut are broadcast spawners that release large numbers of eggs into deep water, generally spawning in late winter and spring in deep offshore holes and along the continental slope. The eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift in deep water; the larvae undergo the flatfish transformation (one eye migrating to the other side) as they develop. Halibut are slow-growing and late-maturing, which makes them vulnerable to overfishing.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Atlantic halibut are a cold-water marine groundfish of deep offshore and Gulf of Maine bottoms.

## Fun fact / angler note

The Atlantic halibut is the giant of the flatfish world — historically, fish of several hundred pounds were recorded, dwarfing the fluke and flounder most anglers catch. Because U.S. stocks are at low levels and strictly managed, a legal halibut is a rare and regulated prize; always confirm the current (often very small) bag limit before keeping one.

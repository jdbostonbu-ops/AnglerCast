# Redear Sunfish (Lepomis microlophus)

Also known as: shellcracker, redear, cherry gill, stumpknocker, sun perch.

## What is a redear sunfish?

The redear sunfish is a larger-growing sunfish best known by its nickname "shellcracker," for its habit of crushing snails and small mussels. It resembles a bluegill but has a black ear flap edged in bright red or orange (on males) and lacks the bluegill's dark dorsal-fin spot and bold side bars. Redear are a southern species widely introduced elsewhere, living in warm, clear lakes, ponds, and reservoirs with abundant vegetation. They tend to stay near the bottom rather than up in the weeds, grow larger than most sunfish, and are prized as a hard-fighting, great-eating panfish.

## What does a redear sunfish eat?

Redear are specialized mollusk feeders — their common name "shellcracker" comes from the strong throat (pharyngeal) teeth they use to crush snails and small clams and mussels, which make up much of their diet. They also eat aquatic insects, larvae, and small crustaceans. Their snail-eating habit is useful: it helps control snails that host fish parasites.

## When is a redear sunfish in season?

Redear are caught spring through fall, with the spring spawn being a prime time. They're usually managed under general panfish/sunfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a redear sunfish spawn?

Redear sunfish spawn in spring and early summer, generally a bit earlier than bluegill, nesting in small colonies in the shallows. The male fans out a nest and guards the eggs and fry, like other sunfish. Where they share water with bluegill, redear sometimes hybridize with them.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Redear sunfish live in warm, clear, vegetated freshwater lakes, ponds, and reservoirs.

## Fun fact / angler note

"Shellcracker" is literal — redear have powerful grinding teeth in their throat for crushing snail and mussel shells, and in waters with invasive snails or mussels they can grow exceptionally large on that rich diet. Because they feed on the bottom rather than up in the weeds, anglers target them with bait fished near the bottom, and a big redear is one of the strongest-pulling panfish you can hook.

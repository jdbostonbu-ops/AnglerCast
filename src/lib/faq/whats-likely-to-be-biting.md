# What's Likely to Be Biting

AnglerCast cannot tell you what is biting right now. No app can — fish move constantly and what they eat changes by the hour. What AnglerCast can tell you what's in season; which species have historically been recorded in your area in this month, and how strong that pattern is. That is a better starting point than a guess.

## Use the species search as a scan

Open the Saltwater or Freshwater page, pick your coordinates and the current month, and try several species in a row. Striped bass first, then bluefish, then fluke, and so on. As you go, write down the sighting rate and the confidence flag for each species. The species with high sighting rates and moderate-to-high confidence are the ones that have shown up in this area in this month, year after year. They are the species worth targeting today.

Species with low sighting rates or low confidence are not impossible — they are just historically uncommon in this area in this month. If you have a personal connection to one of them, go for it, but plan around the species that the data actually supports.

## Read the map alongside the rate

The occurrence map shows where the records cluster. Two species can both have a 30% May sighting rate, but one might have all its records in a small bay while the other is spread across the whole region. Zoom out on the map to see the broader pattern. A tight cluster near your launch point is a stronger signal than a wide scatter.

## Conditions decide the day

Even when the historical record says a species should be around, today's conditions decide whether it bites. A spawning species that should be active in May might be sluggish during a cold snap. A summer species might shut off when the water temperature spikes too high. Use the Explore page to check the live wind, water temperature, tide, and current for the spot you are heading to.

The species that is most likely biting today is usually the one with a strong historical pattern AND conditions that match what that species likes. AnglerCast gives you both halves — you read them together.

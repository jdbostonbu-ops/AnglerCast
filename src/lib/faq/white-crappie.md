# White Crappie (Pomoxis annularis)

Also known as: crappie, calico bass, papermouth, specks, silver crappie.

## What is a white crappie?

The white crappie is the close cousin of the black crappie — a deep-bodied, silvery panfish in the sunfish family and one of the best-eating freshwater fish. It differs from the black crappie in having its dark markings arranged in faint vertical bars (rather than a scattered speckle pattern) and in tolerating more turbid, warmer water. White crappie thrive in slightly murkier lakes, reservoirs, and slow rivers, schooling around brush, timber, and structure. Like all crappie they have soft, papery mouths and are a beloved spring and ice-fishing target.

## What does a white crappie eat?

White crappie eat zooplankton and insects when young and become efficient fish-eaters as they grow — by a few inches long, small fish (especially minnows and young shad, and even young bluegill or perch) make up a large part of their diet, along with aquatic insects. They feed most actively at dawn and dusk, suspending near cover to ambush baitfish.

## When is a white crappie in season?

Crappie are caught year-round, with the spring pre-spawn and spawn most popular, plus strong winter ice fishing. They're managed under panfish or crappie-specific rules (sometimes with size and bag limits) set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a white crappie spawn?

White crappie spawn in spring, generally when water reaches about 56–66°F, moving into shallow areas near brush and cover. The male builds and guards a nest, often in loose colonies, much like other sunfish. They are prolific and can become very abundant — sometimes overpopulating and stunting in waters without enough predators.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. White crappie live in freshwater lakes, reservoirs, and slow rivers, tolerating more turbid water than black crappie.

## Fun fact / angler note

The easiest way to tell the two crappies apart: white crappie wear faint vertical bars, while black crappie are covered in a random scatter of speckles. White crappie handle muddier, warmer water better, so in turbid reservoirs they often outnumber blacks. Like their cousin they're "papermouths," so a gentle hookset and steady pressure keep them buttoned — and a mess of crappie fillets is hard to beat at the table.

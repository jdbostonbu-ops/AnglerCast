# Blueback Herring (Alosa aestivalis)

Also known as: river herring (shared with alewife), blueback, glut herring, summer herring.

## What is a blueback herring?

The blueback herring is a small, silvery, anadromous member of the herring family that — together with the very similar alewife — is collectively called "river herring." Named for its bluish back, it lives most of its life in the ocean and returns to fresh water to spawn, ranging along the Atlantic coast from Nova Scotia to northern Florida and most numerous from Chesapeake Bay south. Blueback herring are so similar to alewives that even biologists can misidentify them by appearance alone, and the two are managed together. They are an important forage fish for many larger predators.

## What does a blueback herring eat?

Blueback herring are plankton feeders, eating zooplankton such as copepods along with other small invertebrates, fish eggs, and tiny fish. They feed in the water column in schools, and serve as forage for striped bass, bluefish, and other predators and seabirds.

## When is a blueback herring in season?

Blueback herring are best known for their spring spawning runs. Like alewives, many river herring populations have declined sharply and are protected with strict limits or closures; seasons and limits are set by state agencies and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a blueback herring spawn?

Blueback herring are anadromous and ascend coastal rivers in spring to spawn in fresh water, typically a bit later than alewives — in the Chesapeake region they appear around April. They favor swifter-flowing rivers and tributaries with harder bottoms for spawning. After spawning, adults migrate quickly back to sea, and juveniles use tidal freshwater nursery areas before moving downstream. Blueback herring spawn repeatedly over several years (roughly ages 3–6).

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both. The blueback herring is anadromous: it spawns in fresh water (favoring swift rivers and tributaries) but lives most of its life in the ocean.

## Fun fact / angler note

Blueback herring and alewives run up the rivers together in spring as "river herring," and telling them apart often requires checking the color of the belly lining or counting fine details — even experts can mix them up. Their spring runs are a vital pulse of food for the whole coastal ecosystem, which is why both species are a focus of restoration efforts like dam removals and fish ladders.

# Albacore Tuna (Thunnus alalunga)

Also known as: albacore, longfin tuna, white tuna, "chicken of the sea."

## What is an albacore tuna?

The albacore is a streamlined pelagic tuna instantly recognized by its extremely long pectoral fins, which reach well past the middle of the body. It is metallic dark blue above and silvery below, smaller on average than bluefin or yellowfin. Albacore are highly migratory, found in warm and temperate offshore waters worldwide, and reach the Northeast offshore grounds seasonally. They are best known commercially as the source of premium canned "white" tuna, and they are also a strong-fighting offshore sportfish.

## What does an albacore tuna eat?

Albacore are active open-water predators feeding on small fish, squid, and crustaceans. They chase schooling baitfish and squid through the water column, hunting with the speed and stamina typical of tunas. They often travel in large schools, sometimes mixed with other tuna species.

## When is an albacore tuna in season?

Albacore appear off the Northeast seasonally in the warmer months on offshore grounds as warm water moves in. They are managed federally by NOAA Fisheries and internationally by ICCAT, with permits and limits that change from year to year. Always check current federal regulations and permit requirements before targeting them.

## When does an albacore tuna spawn?

Albacore are batch spawners that release pelagic eggs into warm offshore waters, spawning in tropical and subtropical seas (not in the cold Northeast). Eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift in warm water, and the young grow in offshore waters before recruiting to the migratory population.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Albacore tuna are a highly migratory open-ocean (pelagic) marine species of warm and temperate offshore waters.

## Fun fact / angler note

Those remarkably long pectoral fins are the quick way to identify an albacore — no other Atlantic tuna has fins that long. Albacore is the only tuna that can legally be labeled "white meat tuna" in U.S. canning, which is why it's the premium canned product. On rod and reel they're fast, schooling fighters that test light offshore tackle.

# Pollock (Pollachius virens)

Also known as: Atlantic pollock, saithe, Boston bluefish, coalfish.

## What is a pollock?

The pollock is a fast-swimming North Atlantic groundfish in the cod family, found from the Gulf of Maine and Georges Bank northward. Unlike its bottom-hugging cod and haddock relatives, pollock are more active in the water column and often chase prey well off the bottom, sometimes near the surface. They have a greenish back, silvery sides, a pointed snout, a projecting lower jaw, and a small or absent chin barbel. Pollock are a strong-fighting, accessible groundfish caught both inshore around structure and offshore.

## What does a pollock eat?

Pollock are active predators that feed more on fish and swimming prey than the more bottom-bound gadoids. Their diet includes small schooling fish (such as herring and sand lance), along with krill, shrimp, amphipods, and other crustaceans. Younger pollock eat more small crustaceans; larger fish become more fish-eating.

## When is a pollock in season?

Pollock are a cold-water groundfish present in New England waters, often more available inshore in the cooler months (late fall through winter is a known period of higher catch rates in places). The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and the New England Fishery Management Council, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a pollock spawn?

Pollock are broadcast spawners that release pelagic eggs into the water column, generally spawning in late fall and winter on offshore banks and ledges. Eggs and larvae drift with currents, and juveniles often move into inshore and coastal waters — including around rocks, jetties, and harbors — before moving offshore as they grow.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Pollock are a marine groundfish of the cold North Atlantic, living from inshore structure to offshore banks.

## Fun fact / angler note

Pollock punch above their weight as gamefish — because they feed up in the water column and fight hard, they're a favorite of jig and inshore anglers, and small "harbor pollock" are commonly caught right off jetties and piers. The name "Boston bluefish" in the market refers to pollock, not actual bluefish.

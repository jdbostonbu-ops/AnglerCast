# Spiny Dogfish (Squalus acanthias)

Also known as: dogfish, spurdog, mud shark, piked dogfish, grayfish, "rock salmon" (in UK fish and chips).

## What is a spiny dogfish?

The spiny dogfish is a small, slender, abundant shark of cool temperate waters, found in the northwest Atlantic from Newfoundland to Cape Hatteras. It is slate gray to brownish above (often with scattered small white spots) and pale below, identifiable by two dorsal fins each preceded by a sharp spine and the absence of an anal fin. Dogfish form very large schools, often segregated by size and sex, and migrate seasonally. They are one of the most common fish encountered by Northeast anglers and a managed commercial species.

## What does a spiny dogfish eat?

Spiny dogfish are opportunistic predators that hunt in packs. They feed on small schooling fish (such as herring, mackerel, and sand lance), along with squid, octopus, crabs, shrimp, jellyfish, and other invertebrates. Fishermen historically named them "dogfish" for the way their schools chase bait like packs of hounds.

## When is a spiny dogfish in season?

Spiny dogfish are present in Northeast waters seasonally, often very abundant inshore in the cooler parts of the warm season as their schools migrate through. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission, with limits that change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a spiny dogfish spawn?

Spiny dogfish are ovoviviparous — eggs develop inside the female and she gives birth to live young ("pups"). They have one of the longest gestation periods of any vertebrate, around 18–24 months, producing a small litter of pups. They are extremely slow to mature (females may not mature until around 18–20 years) and long-lived, which makes the population slow to recover from overfishing.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Spiny dogfish are a marine shark of cool coastal and shelf waters, from inshore to several hundred feet deep.

## Fun fact / angler note

Watch the spines — a hooked dogfish can arch its back to jab the two mild-venom spines near its dorsal fins, so handle them carefully. Though often considered a nuisance when they swarm a fishing spot, they're a legitimate food fish (the classic "rock salmon" of British fish and chips) and an important part of the ecosystem.

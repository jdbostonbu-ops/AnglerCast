# Cisco (Coregonus artedi)

Also known as: lake herring, tullibee, northern cisco, chub, lake cisco.

## What is a cisco?

The cisco, often called lake herring, is a slender, silvery freshwater fish in the whitefish subfamily of the salmon/trout family (Salmonidae) — despite the "herring" nickname, it is not a true herring at all. It has the salmonid's small adipose fin and a streamlined body, usually 11–15 inches. Cisco are a cold-water, open-water (pelagic) schooling fish of large, deep, cold northern lakes, where they're a crucial forage species for predators like lake trout, northern pike, and walleye. They're also commercially and recreationally valued, and a popular target through the ice in some northern lakes.

## What does a cisco eat?

Cisco feed primarily on zooplankton, filtering tiny drifting crustaceans from open water. Adults also eat aquatic insect larvae, emerging mayflies and stoneflies, and other small bottom and surface invertebrates. They sometimes move into the shallows in summer to feast on hatching insects.

## When is a cisco in season?

Cisco are caught in cold months and are a notable ice-fishing target on northern lakes, as well as during their late-fall spawning concentrations. Seasons and limits are set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a cisco spawn?

Cisco spawn in late fall and early winter, as water temperatures drop toward about 40°F. They gather in schools and broadcast their eggs over clean bottom in the shallows of lakes (and sometimes in open water or streams) — no nest, no parental care. The eggs settle into rocky crevices and develop slowly over winter, hatching as the ice begins to thaw in spring.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Cisco live in cold, deep freshwater lakes (and some large northern rivers).

## Fun fact / angler note

The "lake herring" name is a bit of a trick — cisco are actually salmonids, cousins of trout and whitefish, not real herring. They're a keystone forage fish: healthy cisco populations help grow trophy lake trout and pike, so their presence is a sign of a productive cold-water lake. As table fare they're well regarded, especially smoked.

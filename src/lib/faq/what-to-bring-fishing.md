# What to Bring Fishing

There is a difference between what you have to bring (legally) and what you should bring (to actually catch fish and stay comfortable). This doc covers both. The exact list depends on where you fish, how you fish, and how long you are out, but the categories are universal.

## The legal essentials

A fishing license — required almost everywhere. Recreational fishing licenses are issued by each state's Fish and Wildlife agency and are easy to get online, at sporting goods stores, tackle shops, marinas, or by phone. Freshwater and saltwater are usually separate licenses; many states offer a combination license that covers both. Short-term licenses (1, 3, or 7 days) are common for non-residents.

Some states require a saltwater recreational registration instead of, or in addition to, a license — Virginia, New Jersey, New York, Maine, and others use a free or low-cost registration system. A saltwater recreational fishing license or registration from any state, except Hawaii, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands, automatically registers you in the federal National Saltwater Angler Registry for fishing in federal waters (typically beyond 3 nautical miles). Highly migratory species — tuna, marlin, sailfish, swordfish, sharks, billfish — require a separate federal permit from NOAA, even for recreational anglers.

Carry your license with you when you fish. In most states, "take" is defined broadly enough that you need a license even to attempt to catch a fish, not just to keep one. Wardens do check.

A current regulations summary — your state agency publishes one each year, and it covers size limits, bag limits, seasons, slot limits, and any species-specific rules for your waters. Keep a printed copy or a saved digital one on your phone. Regulations change; assume last year's rules are out of date.

## Tackle and fishing gear

A rod and reel matched to what you're targeting. A light spinning setup works for most freshwater panfish and small bass; medium spinning or baitcasting handles most inshore saltwater and larger freshwater fish; heavy gear is for offshore and big game. Match your line weight to your rod — too light and you lose fish, too heavy and you spook them.

Terminal tackle in a small organized box — hooks of a few sizes, sinkers in a few weights, swivels, leaders, a few floats, and a small selection of lures or jigs appropriate for your species and water. You do not need a giant tackle bag; you need the right small selection.

Bait — live, fresh, or artificial. What works depends on the species and the water. Local bait shops are the most honest source for what is currently producing in your area.

A landing net, pliers or a hook remover, a fish gripper, and a measuring device to check legal size before you decide whether to keep or release. A small towel for handling fish, and a stringer or cooler with ice if you plan to keep what you catch and the regulations allow it.

## Personal gear

Sun protection — wide-brim hat, polarized sunglasses (they cut surface glare and help you see fish), sunscreen, and long sleeves on bright days. Polarized glasses are not optional on the water; they make you a better angler and they protect your eyes from UV and from errant hooks.

Water and food. Bring more than you think you need. Dehydration sneaks up on you on the water, especially in wind and sun.

Clothing layered for the actual conditions. The temperature on the water is almost always cooler than at the launch, and the wind makes it feel cooler still. A rain shell handles unexpected weather. If you might end up wet, bring a dry change in a sealed bag.

A small dry bag or waterproof case for your phone, wallet, keys, and license. Phones drop overboard; protect them.

## Boat-specific extras (if you're not bank fishing)

Your safety gear from the safety doc — PFDs, throwable, distress signals, fire extinguisher if required, sound-signaling device, VHF radio. A boat hook, an anchor and line, a paddle in case the engine fails, and a small toolkit with the basics for your motor. A first-aid kit. Extra fuel if your trip is long.

## The checklist habit

The angler who never forgets the net is the angler who uses a checklist. Build a simple list — your version, for your boat or your bank-fishing setup — and run it before every trip. Forgetting your license or your needle-nose pliers is annoying. Forgetting your PFD or your VHF can be dangerous.

# Scup (Stenotomus chrysops)

Also known as: porgy, northern porgy, fairmaid.

## What is a scup?

Scup, widely called porgy, are a deep-bodied, silvery panfish-style saltwater species common in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic, ranging from roughly Newfoundland to Cape Hatteras and most abundant from Cape Cod to the Carolinas. They are schooling bottom fish that gather over a range of bottoms — sand, mud, mussel beds, and around structure. Scup are a popular, accessible recreational fish, often caught in good numbers from boats, piers, and shorelines.

## What does a scup eat?

Scup are bottom feeders with small mouths and feed on a variety of small invertebrates. Their diet includes worms (polychaetes), small crustaceans, amphipods, mollusks, sand dollars, and other small benthic organisms, along with bits of vegetation. They forage along the bottom, picking at small prey.

## When is a scup in season?

Scup are an inshore warm-season presence in the Northeast, moving into coastal waters and bays as it warms and offshore as it cools. Recreational seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and individual states and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a scup spawn?

Scup spawn once a year, in late spring and early summer (generally May into July), in inshore and nearshore coastal waters and bays. The eggs are pelagic — they drift in the water column — and hatch quickly. Juveniles use estuaries and bays as nursery habitat. Scup grow slowly and can live to around 20 years.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Scup are a marine species living in coastal ocean waters, bays, and over the continental shelf, using estuaries as nursery habitat.

## Fun fact / angler note

Scup are a favorite for introducing new anglers to saltwater fishing — they school in large numbers, bite readily on simple bait rigs, and are good eating. Their sharp dorsal spines call for a little care when handling.

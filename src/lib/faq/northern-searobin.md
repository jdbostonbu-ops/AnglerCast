# Northern Searobin (Prionotus carolinus)

Also known as: sea robin, common sea robin, gurnard.

## What is a northern searobin?

The northern searobin is an odd, distinctive bottom fish with a broad, bony, spiny head, a tapering body, blue eyes, and large wing-like pectoral fins it spreads like a bird's wings. It ranges in the western Atlantic from Nova Scotia to Florida and into the Gulf of Mexico, living over sandy bottoms from estuaries to the edge of the continental shelf. The lower few rays of its pectoral/pelvic fins are separated into finger-like "legs" that it uses to walk along the bottom and stir up sediment to find food. Searobins are often caught incidentally and put up a scrappy fight.

## What does a northern searobin eat?

Northern searobins are bottom feeders that use their finger-like fin rays to probe and dig through sand for prey. Their diet includes shrimp, crabs and other crustaceans, squid, bivalves, marine worms, and small fish. They literally "walk" the bottom and kick up sediment to expose hidden prey.

## When is a northern searobin in season?

Northern searobins are a warm-season inshore presence in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic, moving inshore in spring and summer and offshore or south as it cools. There usually aren't specific recreational limits, but regulations can vary by state, so check current local rules. They are frequently caught while fishing sandy bottoms for other species.

## When does a northern searobin spawn?

Northern searobins spawn in summer, with a prolonged season because only a few eggs ripen at a time. Fertilization is external, the eggs are pelagic and tiny, and they hatch quickly (around 60 hours in warm water). There is no parental care. During mating season males produce a distinctive staccato call, separate from their usual grunting.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Northern searobins are a coastal marine and estuarine species over sandy bottoms, from the shallows to the shelf edge.

## Fun fact / angler note

Searobins really do "walk" and "talk" — they use separate finger-like fin rays to creep along the bottom hunting food, and they produce loud drumming and grunting sounds by vibrating their swim bladder (you can often hear them on deck). Long dismissed as a nuisance catch, they're actually good eating, with firm white flesh sometimes compared to whiting.

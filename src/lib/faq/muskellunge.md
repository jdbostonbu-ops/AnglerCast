# Muskellunge (Esox masquinongy)

Also known as: muskie, musky, lunge, 'the fish of 10,000 casts'.

## What is a muskellunge?

The muskellunge is the largest member of the pike family and one of the largest freshwater gamefish in North America, capable of reaching six feet and well over 30 pounds. It is a long, powerful ambush predator with a duckbill snout and a mouthful of teeth, patterned with dark spots, bars, or markings on a lighter background (the reverse of a northern pike). Muskie live in larger lakes and rivers with good structure and prey, holding near weeds, drop-offs, and cover. Famously hard to catch, they've earned the nickname "the fish of 10,000 casts," and a trophy muskie is a top-tier freshwater trophy.

## What does a muskellunge eat?

Muskellunge are apex freshwater ambush predators with big appetites. They eat mostly fish — including suckers, ciscoes, yellow perch, shad, and other sizable prey — and will also take frogs, crayfish, ducklings, muskrats, and other small animals. They prefer large meals and can eat prey a third or more of their own length, ambushing from cover with an explosive strike.

## When is a muskellunge in season?

Muskie are caught spring through fall, with fall often prized for trophy fish. They are tightly managed, typically with high minimum size limits and very low bag limits (often one fish, and largely a catch-and-release fishery), set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a muskellunge spawn?

Muskellunge spawn in spring, somewhat later than northern pike, when they move into weedy shallows and bays. They scatter adhesive eggs over vegetation and bottom with no nest and no parental care. Where pike and muskie spawn in the same waters, they occasionally interbreed naturally to produce tiger muskellunge. Muskie are slow-growing and long-lived, which makes them sensitive to overharvest — a major reason for catch-and-release.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Muskellunge live in larger freshwater lakes and rivers.

## Fun fact / angler note

Muskie are legendary for how hard they are to hook — "the fish of 10,000 casts" is only a slight exaggeration, and dedicated muskie anglers may fish for days for a single follow or strike. They're also known for following a lure right to the boat before turning away, which is why anglers finish every retrieve with a figure-eight beside the boat to trigger a strike. Because they grow slowly and live long, muskie fishing is overwhelmingly catch-and-release.

# Witch Flounder (Glyptocephalus cynoglossus)

Also known as: grey sole, gray sole, pole flounder, Craig fluke.

## What is a witch flounder?

The witch flounder, sold at market as "grey sole," is a small-mouthed, right-eyed flatfish of the deep, cold North Atlantic, found in the Gulf of Maine and on offshore banks over soft mud bottoms in deep water. It has a slender, oval body, small head, and a small mouth, and is a uniform brownish-gray. Witch flounder live deeper than most of the inshore flatfish and are primarily a commercial groundfish — they're highly regarded as one of the finest-eating flatfish, which is why "grey sole" commands a premium.

## What does a witch flounder eat?

Witch flounder have small mouths and feed on small bottom invertebrates. Their diet is made up of polychaete worms, small crustaceans (amphipods), brittle stars, small mollusks, and other tiny benthic animals. They pick small prey off and out of the soft, deep mud bottoms where they live.

## When is a witch flounder in season?

Witch flounder are a deepwater groundfish present in the Gulf of Maine and on offshore banks year-round in suitable depths. The fishery is managed by NOAA Fisheries and the New England Fishery Management Council, with limits that change from year to year. They are mostly a commercial catch rather than a common rod-and-reel target. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a witch flounder spawn?

Witch flounder are broadcast spawners that release pelagic (drifting) eggs into the water column, generally spawning in late spring and summer in deep water. Their larvae are notable for having an unusually long pelagic (drifting) larval stage — among the longest of the flatfish — before settling to the deep mud bottom. Witch flounder are slow-growing and relatively long-lived.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Witch flounder are a deepwater marine flatfish of soft mud bottoms in the Gulf of Maine and offshore North Atlantic.

## Fun fact / angler note

As "grey sole," the witch flounder is considered one of the most delicious flatfish on the market, with delicate, sweet fillets. Its larvae drift in the open water for an exceptionally long time before settling — one of the longest larval journeys among flatfish. Because they live deep, anglers rarely catch them inshore; they're mainly a prized commercial fish.

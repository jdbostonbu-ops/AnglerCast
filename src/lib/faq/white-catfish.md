# White Catfish (Ameiurus catus)

Also known as: white bullhead, forked-tail bullhead, white cat.

## What is a white catfish?

The white catfish is a medium-sized catfish native to the Atlantic slope, found especially in coastal rivers, tidal creeks, ponds, and lakes from the Northeast southward. It's a bit of an in-between fish: larger than the bullheads but smaller than a channel catfish, with a moderately forked tail (more forked than a bullhead's square tail, less than a channel cat's deep fork), a broad head, white chin barbels, and a bluish-gray to slate body with a pale belly. White catfish tolerate brackish, tidal water better than most catfish, which is why they're often found in the tidal stretches of coastal rivers. They're a hardy, willing catch and good eating.

## What does a white catfish eat?

White catfish are opportunistic omnivores and scavengers. They eat aquatic insects, crayfish and other crustaceans, mollusks, worms, small fish, fish eggs, and plant material, and they readily scavenge. Like other catfish, they rely heavily on smell and taste to find food, feeding actively in low light and after dark.

## When is a white catfish in season?

White catfish are caught spring through fall, including in tidal rivers and creeks. They're usually managed under general or catfish rules set by state agencies and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a white catfish spawn?

White catfish spawn in late spring and early summer, building a nest in the bottom (often in a depression near cover) in shallow water. As with other members of the catfish family, the parents guard the eggs and the young after hatching. They can live well over a decade.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Mostly freshwater, but notably tolerant of brackish water. White catfish are primarily a freshwater fish that does especially well in the tidal, slightly salty stretches of coastal rivers and estuaries — more salt-tolerant than the bullheads or channel catfish.

## Fun fact / angler note

The white catfish's tail is the giveaway — moderately forked, midway between a bullhead's square tail and a channel cat's deep fork — and its chin barbels are white. Because it handles brackish tidal water, it's a common catch in coastal river systems where you might not expect a catfish.

# Brown Trout (Salmo trutta)

Also known as: brownie, German brown, Loch Leven trout.

## What is a brown trout?

The brown trout is a hardy, wary, cold-water gamefish native to Europe and western Asia, introduced widely across North America in the 1800s and now naturalized in many Northeast streams, rivers, and lakes. It is golden-brown to olive with black and often red or orange spots, many ringed with pale halos, and lacks the marbled back of a brook trout. Brown trout tolerate slightly warmer and less pristine water than brook trout, grow large, and are famously challenging to catch — older fish become nocturnal and selective. They are a premier wild and stocked sportfish.

## What does a brown trout eat?

Brown trout are opportunistic predators whose diet broadens as they grow. Smaller fish eat aquatic and terrestrial insects, larvae, crustaceans, and worms; larger brown trout become strongly piscivorous, eating other fish (including smaller trout), crayfish, and even small mammals or large terrestrial insects. Big browns often feed heavily at night.

## When is a brown trout in season?

Brown trout are a cold-water species most active in cooler conditions; in the Northeast they're targeted in spring and fall and seek cooler water in summer. Seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by state fish and wildlife agencies and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a brown trout spawn?

Brown trout spawn in fall (typically late October into December), when the female digs a gravel redd in clean, well-oxygenated water and buries the fertilized eggs. The eggs incubate over winter and hatch in spring. As with other trout, cold, clean, oxygenated gravel is essential. Brown trout can live to around 20 years and grow large under good conditions.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Brown trout live in cold freshwater streams, rivers, and lakes. (A sea-run form called the "sea trout" exists in some coastal regions, spending time in salt water, but in the Northeast they're managed as a freshwater species.)

## Fun fact / angler note

Brown trout have a reputation as the wiliest of the stream trout — big browns grow old and cagey, feeding mostly after dark and refusing all but the most careful presentations, which makes a trophy brown one of the most respected catches in fly fishing. They were originally brought over from Europe, so every North American brown trout traces back to imported stock.

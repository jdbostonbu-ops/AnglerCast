# Northern Pike (Esox lucius)

Also known as: pike, northern, jack, jackfish, snake, gator.

## What is a northern pike?

The northern pike is a long, powerful, ambush predator with a torpedo-shaped body, a flat duckbill snout, and a mouth full of sharp teeth. It is olive-green to gray, marked with rows of light, bean-shaped spots on a darker background (the reverse of a muskie's pattern). Pike live in freshwater lakes, slow rivers, and weedy bays across the northern hemisphere, holding in or near vegetation where they wait to ambush prey. They grow large, strike explosively, and are a top freshwater predator — a thrilling, hard-fighting gamefish.

## What does a northern pike eat?

Northern pike are voracious, opportunistic ambush predators. They eat mostly fish — including yellow perch, suckers, ciscoes, shad, smaller pike, and almost anything they can grab — and will also take frogs, crayfish, small waterfowl, and even small mammals that enter the water. They lie still among weeds and strike with a sudden lunge, using their teeth to seize prey crosswise before turning it to swallow head-first.

## When is a northern pike in season?

Pike are caught year-round and are a popular open-water and ice-fishing target. They are managed with seasons, size limits, and bag limits set by state agencies (with spawning-season rules on some waters) and subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a northern pike spawn?

Northern pike spawn very early in spring, often right at or just after ice-out, when they move into flooded, weedy shallows, marshes, and backwaters. They scatter their adhesive eggs over vegetation — there is no nest and no parental care — and the eggs stick to plants until they hatch. Pike are among the first fish to spawn each year, taking advantage of cold, high spring water.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Northern pike live in freshwater lakes, rivers, and weedy bays (with some populations tolerating slightly brackish water at range edges, such as the Baltic).

## Fun fact / angler note

You can tell a pike from a muskie by the pattern: northern pike have light spots on a dark body, while muskies have dark markings on a light body. Pike are notorious for their toothy, slashing strikes, so anglers use wire or heavy leaders and long-nose pliers or jaw spreaders for safe unhooking. A big pike erupting from the weeds on a fast-moving lure is one of fresh water's most heart-stopping strikes.

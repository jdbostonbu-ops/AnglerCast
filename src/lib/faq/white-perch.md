# White Perch (Morone americana)

Also known as: silver perch, sea perch, narrow-mouthed bass.

## What is a white perch?

Despite its name, the white perch is not a true perch — it's a temperate bass, in the same genus (Morone) as the striped bass and white bass. It is a small, deep-bodied, silvery fish, greenish to gray above and pale below, without the bold stripes of its striped bass cousin. White perch live in coastal rivers, estuaries, ponds, and lakes from the Atlantic coast inland, and they tolerate fresh, brackish, and salt water. They school in large numbers, bite readily, and are good eating, but they can become overabundant and stunted in some waters, and are considered invasive in parts of the Great Lakes.

## What does a white perch eat?

White perch are highly opportunistic, generalist feeders. They eat aquatic insects and larvae, crustaceans, zooplankton, worms, small fish, and fish eggs — including, notably, large amounts of other fish's eggs (and even their own) in spring, which can affect populations of species like walleye and yellow perch. Juvenile alewives and minnows are important food in some waters.

## When is a white perch in season?

White perch are caught spring through fall and through the ice in winter, often in large schools. They're typically managed under general panfish rules, though limits vary by water and state and can change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a white perch spawn?

White perch spawn in spring (generally April–June), moving into the shallows of rivers, the heads of tide, and lake margins. Females broadcast large numbers of sticky (adhesive) eggs over sandy or gravelly bottom, where they are fertilized externally and stick to the substrate — there is no nest and no parental care. A single female can produce tens of thousands to several hundred thousand eggs, which is part of why white perch can become very abundant.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both, in practice. White perch are a temperate bass that lives in fresh, brackish, and coastal salt water — they're equally at home in inland ponds and lakes and in tidal rivers and estuaries, which is why they appear in freshwater settings while being closely related to the saltwater striped bass.

## Fun fact / angler note

The white perch's spring habit of gorging on other fish's eggs (including walleye and yellow perch eggs) means a population boom of white perch can actually suppress other fisheries — a good example of how one species' diet ripples through a whole lake. For anglers, they're a fun, fast, schooling target that bites willingly and tastes great, but in some waters managers encourage keeping them to prevent overcrowding.

# Northern Kingfish (Menticirrhus saxatilis)

Also known as: kingfish, sea mullet, whiting, roundhead, minkfish.

## What is a northern kingfish?

The northern kingfish is a small bottom-dwelling member of the drum family (Sciaenidae), found along the U.S. Atlantic coast from roughly Maine to Florida and into the Gulf, most common in the Mid-Atlantic. It has a slightly elongated, humpbacked body with dark oblique bars, a small chin barbel used to sense food on the bottom, and a high front dorsal fin. Northern kingfish live over sand and sandy-mud bottoms in the surf zone, inlets, and nearshore coastal waters, and they are a popular surf and pier catch.

## What does a northern kingfish eat?

Northern kingfish are bottom feeders that use the chin barbel to locate prey in the sand. Their diet is made up of small bottom invertebrates — shrimp, small crabs, amphipods, worms, mollusks — and small fish. They root along sandy bottoms and in the surf for these prey.

## When is a northern kingfish in season?

Northern kingfish are a warm-season inshore presence in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic, appearing in the surf and nearshore waters in the warmer months and leaving as it cools. Seasons and limits, where they apply, are set by state agencies and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and can change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a northern kingfish spawn?

Northern kingfish spawn in spring through summer in nearshore coastal and estuarine waters. The eggs and larvae are pelagic, and the young use estuaries and nearshore areas as nursery grounds. They are relatively short-lived and fast-growing.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Northern kingfish are a coastal marine species of the surf zone, inlets, and nearshore bottom, using estuaries as nursery habitat.

## Fun fact / angler note

Despite the regal name, the northern kingfish is a modest-sized surf fish — but a scrappy, hard-pulling one that's prized by surfcasters and good on the table. The chin barbel is a giveaway that it's a bottom-feeding drum, related to croaker and spot. In some areas it's confusingly called "whiting," a name also used for other species.

# Round Goby (Neogobius melanostomus)

Also known as: round goby, goby.

## What is a round goby?

The round goby is a small, bottom-dwelling invasive fish native to the Black and Caspian Sea region (the Ponto-Caspian area), introduced to North America — most notably the Great Lakes — through ship ballast water in the early 1990s. It has a soft, scaleless-looking body, a rounded head with frog-like raised eyes, and, most distinctively, fused pelvic fins that form a single suction-cup-like disc on the belly (which lets it cling to rocks and the bottom). A black spot on the rear of the front dorsal fin is another key mark. Round gobies are aggressive, prolific, and highly adaptable, and they've become a major invasive species, disrupting native fish communities — but they've also become an important food source for some predators like smallmouth bass and walleye.

## What does a round goby eat?

Round gobies are aggressive bottom feeders with a broad, opportunistic diet. They eat aquatic insects, small crustaceans, worms, and — significantly — mollusks, including invasive zebra and quagga mussels, which they crush and eat. They also eat the eggs and young of native fish, which is one reason they're so disruptive: they compete with and prey upon native bottom fish.

## When is a round goby in season?

Round gobies are an invasive species, and in many areas there are no protective limits — in fact, anglers are often asked never to release them or use them as bait in new waters, to avoid spreading them further. Regulations and reporting requirements vary by state and are subject to change. Always check current local rules, and never transport live gobies between waters.

## When does a round goby spawn?

Round gobies are prolific spawners, which fuels their invasion success. They can spawn repeatedly through the warm season (multiple times from late spring into summer), and the male guards the eggs, which are attached to hard surfaces like rocks and shells. Their long spawning season, repeated spawning, and nest-guarding all help them multiply rapidly in new waters.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater (and brackish). In North America the round goby is established in fresh water — especially the Great Lakes and connecting rivers — though in its native range it also tolerates brackish water.

## Fun fact / angler note

The round goby is a textbook aquatic invader: it arrived in ballast water, spread explosively, and reshaped Great Lakes food webs. There's a silver lining — gobies eat invasive zebra and quagga mussels, and in turn have become important forage for smallmouth bass and walleye in some lakes. But because they also eat native fish eggs and outcompete native bottom fish, anglers should never release them or move them to new waters.

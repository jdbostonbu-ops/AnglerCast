# Swordfish (Xiphias gladius)

Also known as: broadbill, broadbill swordfish.

## What is a swordfish?

The swordfish is a large, powerful, highly migratory billfish named for its long, flat, sword-like bill (about a third of its body length), which it uses to slash and stun prey. It has a stout, rounded body, a tall first dorsal fin, and large eyes, and unlike marlins it lacks pelvic fins and scales as an adult. Swordfish range across warm and temperate oceans worldwide, including the Northeast offshore canyons, and they make deep daily vertical migrations. They are a top offshore gamefish and a valuable commercial species.

## What does a swordfish eat?

Swordfish are aggressive predators that feed on fish and squid. They use their bill to slash through schools of prey, stunning or injuring fish, then circle back to eat. Their diet includes squid, mackerel, herring, hakes, lanternfishes, and other open-water and deepwater fish. They feed at the surface at night and at depth during the day.

## When is a swordfish in season?

Swordfish are present off the Northeast on offshore grounds seasonally, mainly in the warmer months; they are famously caught by deep-dropping during the day and by drifting baits at night. They are managed federally by NOAA Fisheries and internationally by ICCAT, with permits and limits that change from year to year. Always check current federal regulations and permit requirements before targeting them.

## When does a swordfish spawn?

Swordfish are batch spawners that release pelagic eggs into warm offshore waters. Spawning occurs in warm seas, year-round in tropical waters and seasonally toward the edges of the range. Eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift in warm water, and the young grow rapidly in offshore waters.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Swordfish are a highly migratory open-ocean (pelagic) marine species of warm and temperate offshore waters.

## Fun fact / angler note

Swordfish have special heat-exchanging tissue that warms their eyes and brain, sharpening their vision in the cold, dark depths where they hunt by day — they can dive thousands of feet and tolerate near-freezing water. Daytime deep-dropping for swordfish has become a specialized offshore pursuit, and a big broadbill is one of the most respected catches in big-game fishing.

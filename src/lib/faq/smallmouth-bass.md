# Smallmouth Bass (Micropterus dolomieu)

Also known as: smallmouth, smallie, bronzeback, brown bass, black bass.

## What is a smallmouth bass?

The smallmouth bass is a powerful, acrobatic member of the sunfish family, widely regarded as one of the hardest-fighting freshwater fish for its size. It is bronze to brownish-green (earning the nickname "bronzeback"), often with dark vertical bars, and its mouth — unlike the largemouth's — does not extend past the eye. Smallmouth prefer cooler, cleaner, rockier water than largemouth: clear lakes with rocky structure and cool, flowing rivers and streams. Their populations are patchier because of these stricter habitat needs, but where they thrive, they're a premier sportfish.

## What does a smallmouth bass eat?

Smallmouth bass are carnivorous predators with a strong preference for crayfish, along with small fish (such as minnows, darters, and young of other species) and aquatic and terrestrial insects. Crayfish are such a staple that crayfish-imitating lures are a smallmouth classic. They feed actively around rocky structure and current.

## When is a smallmouth bass in season?

Smallmouth are caught spring through fall, with strong feeding in the pre-spawn spring and again in fall; in summer larger fish often move to deeper, cooler water. Seasons, size limits, and bag limits (and spawn-season protections in some states) are set by state fish and wildlife agencies and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a smallmouth bass spawn?

Smallmouth spawn in late spring to early summer, typically a bit earlier than largemouth, when water reaches roughly 59–64°F. The male builds a nest, usually over coarse gravel, rock rubble, or near a boulder or stump, in water from less than a foot to 10+ feet deep, and guards the eggs and fry aggressively. Not all males nest every year, and larger, experienced males tend to have the most successful nests.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Smallmouth bass live in cool, clear freshwater lakes and rivers with rocky structure and good water quality.

## Fun fact / angler note

Pound for pound, smallmouth are often called the scrappiest fighter in fresh water — they run hard and leap repeatedly, far above their weight class. They love crayfish and rocky current, so anglers target them around boulders, ledges, and gravel. A river smallmouth on light tackle is one of the most exciting fights in freshwater angling.

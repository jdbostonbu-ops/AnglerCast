# Bigeye Tuna (Thunnus obesus)

Also known as: bigeye, ahi (shared with yellowfin), 'ahi po'onui.

## What is a bigeye tuna?

The bigeye tuna is a deep-bodied, powerful pelagic predator named for its notably large eyes, which are an adaptation for hunting in dim light at depth. It resembles yellowfin but is stockier, with shorter fins and larger eyes. Bigeye are highly migratory and found in warm and temperate offshore waters worldwide, reaching the Northeast canyons and offshore grounds seasonally. They are a prized offshore gamefish and a high-value commercial and sushi-market species (also sold as "ahi").

## What does a bigeye tuna eat?

Bigeye are deep-diving predators that feed on fish, squid, and crustaceans. They are known for making deep daytime dives to follow the "deep scattering layer" of prey that rises and falls in the water column, returning to shallower water at night. Their large eyes help them hunt in the low light of deeper water.

## When is a bigeye tuna in season?

Bigeye appear off the Northeast seasonally, mainly in the warmer months on offshore canyon grounds, often biting around dawn and dusk and at night when they come shallower. They are managed federally by NOAA Fisheries and internationally by ICCAT, with permits and limits that change from year to year. Always check current federal regulations and permit requirements before targeting them.

## When does a bigeye tuna spawn?

Bigeye are batch spawners that release pelagic eggs into warm open-ocean waters, spawning in tropical waters where conditions are warm enough, often more than once per season. Eggs and larvae are pelagic and drift, and the young grow in warm offshore waters.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Bigeye tuna are a highly migratory open-ocean (pelagic) marine species, ranging deep in warm and temperate offshore waters.

## Fun fact / angler note

Bigeye earn their name — their oversized eyes let them hunt deep, where light is scarce, and they're famous for following prey down hundreds of feet during the day. Offshore anglers often catch them around first light and after dark, when bigeye move up in the water column. Like yellowfin, they're top-grade "ahi" at market.

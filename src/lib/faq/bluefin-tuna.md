# Bluefin Tuna (Thunnus thynnus)

Also known as: Atlantic bluefin, giant bluefin, tunny, bluefin.

## What is a bluefin tuna?

The Atlantic bluefin tuna is one of the largest and fastest fish in the ocean — a powerful, warm-blooded pelagic predator that can exceed 1,000 pounds and live up to about 40 years. Metallic blue above and silvery below, built like a torpedo with a crescent tail, bluefin are highly migratory, crossing entire ocean basins, and they seasonally come close to shore off the Northeast. They are among the most prized and valuable fish in the world, both as a sportfishing trophy and on the sushi market, and they are intensively managed due to a history of overfishing.

## What does a bluefin tuna eat?

Bluefin are visual, high-speed predators. They feed on small schooling fish such as herring, mackerel, sand lance, anchovies, and hakes, along with squid and crustaceans like red crabs. They school by size — sometimes alongside other tunas — and chase down prey with bursts of speed, aided by their warm-blooded physiology that lets their muscles work harder than most fish.

## When is a bluefin tuna in season?

Bluefin tuna appear off the Northeast seasonally, generally in the warmer months when they follow bait inshore and onto offshore grounds. Bluefin are tightly regulated under international and federal management (NOAA Fisheries and ICCAT), with permits, size classes, and quotas that change from year to year. Always check current federal regulations and permit requirements before targeting them.

## When does a bluefin tuna spawn?

Bluefin are batch spawners that release pelagic eggs into warm offshore waters. The western Atlantic stock spawns primarily in the Gulf of Mexico in spring (around March–June), while the eastern stock spawns in the Mediterranean. A large female can produce tens of millions of eggs in a season. Eggs and larvae are pelagic, drifting in warm water, and the young grow rapidly.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Bluefin tuna are a highly migratory open-ocean (pelagic) marine species, ranging across the Atlantic and seasonally near the coast.

## Fun fact / angler note

Bluefin tuna are partially warm-blooded — they retain metabolic heat to keep their muscles, eyes, and brain warmer than the surrounding water, which fuels their incredible speed and lets them range into cold seas. Hooking a giant bluefin is one of the ultimate big-game angling challenges, and the fishery is strictly permitted and quota-managed.

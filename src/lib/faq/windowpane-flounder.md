# Windowpane Flounder (Scophthalmus aquosus)

Also known as: windowpane, sand dab, sundial, spotted flounder, brill.

## What is a windowpane flounder?

The windowpane is a thin, nearly translucent left-eyed flatfish found along the U.S. Atlantic coast from the Gulf of St. Lawrence to Florida, common in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic. Its name comes from how thin its body is — held up to the light, the flesh is so thin it seems you could almost see through it. It is roughly round in outline, mottled brown with small spots, and lives on sand and sandy-mud bottoms in coastal and estuarine waters. Because of its thinness it has little food value and is mostly a catch-and-release or bycatch species for anglers.

## What does a windowpane flounder eat?

Windowpane are ambush predators that, despite their thin bodies, are fairly aggressive feeders. They eat small fish, shrimp, mysids, small crustaceans, and other small bottom and near-bottom prey, lying camouflaged on the bottom and darting up to grab passing prey.

## When is a windowpane flounder in season?

Windowpane are present in Northeast coastal and estuarine waters and are most often encountered inshore in the warmer months. They are managed by NOAA Fisheries and regional councils, but because they have little culinary value they are commonly released. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a windowpane flounder spawn?

Windowpane are broadcast spawners that release pelagic (drifting) eggs into the water column. Spawning occurs over an extended period, generally spring through fall in the Northeast (with peaks in spring and again in late summer/fall depending on area). Eggs and larvae drift, and juveniles settle into coastal and estuarine nursery habitat.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Windowpane are a coastal marine and estuarine flatfish living on sand and sandy-mud bottoms.

## Fun fact / angler note

The windowpane really is named for its translucence — it's one of the thinnest flatfish, which is why anglers usually release it rather than keep it for the table. It's a frequent incidental catch when fishing sandy bottoms for fluke and other flatfish, recognizable by its round shape and thin, spotted body.

# Channel Catfish (Ictalurus punctatus)

Also known as: channel cat, spotted cat, fiddler (small ones), blue channel cat.

## What is a channel catfish?

The channel catfish is the most widespread and popular catfish in North America, a smooth, scaleless fish with long whisker-like barbels, a deeply forked tail, and (especially in younger fish) scattered dark spots on a gray-to-olive body. Native to the central U.S. and widely introduced elsewhere, channel cats live in rivers, lakes, reservoirs, and ponds, preferring areas with some current or structure. They have keen senses of smell and taste — their whole body is covered in taste buds — and feed largely at night. Channel catfish grow large, fight hard, and are one of the most important food and sport fish in fresh water.

## What does a channel catfish eat?

Channel catfish are opportunistic omnivores and scavengers. They eat aquatic insects and larvae, crayfish, mollusks, worms, small fish, and also plant material, seeds, and detritus — and they readily scavenge dead and decaying matter, located by smell. Their willingness to eat almost anything is why anglers catch them on everything from nightcrawlers and cut bait to stinkbait and prepared "dough" baits.

## When is a channel catfish in season?

Channel catfish are caught spring through fall, with warm summer nights especially productive. They're managed with seasons and limits that vary by state and are subject to change. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a channel catfish spawn?

Channel catfish spawn in late spring and early summer, when water warms into the 70s°F. The male selects a secluded, dark, enclosed nest site — an undercut bank, hollow log, hole, or cavity — and after the female lays her sticky egg mass, the male guards and fans the eggs and then the fry. This cavity-nesting, egg-guarding behavior is typical of the catfish family.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Freshwater. Channel catfish live in freshwater rivers, lakes, reservoirs, and ponds (tolerating slightly brackish water in some coastal areas).

## Fun fact / angler note

A catfish is essentially a swimming tongue — channel cats have taste buds all over their skin and barbels, letting them find food by smell and taste in dark or muddy water where sight is useless. That's why scent-heavy baits work so well, and why night fishing is prime time. Handle them carefully: the dorsal and pectoral fins have sharp, locking spines that can deliver a painful jab.

# Fishing Trip Safety

Safety is the first decision you make before every trip, and a few cheap, well-known habits prevent most of the bad outcomes. The U.S. Coast Guard's recreational-boating data is consistent year over year: the majority of drowning victims were not wearing a life jacket, and most of those incidents involved boats that, by the numbers, looked routine — calm water, short trips, experienced operators. The water does not forgive mistakes, so plan as if today is the day you would need every piece of equipment to work.

## Wear a life jacket — actually wear it

A USCG-approved life jacket has to be on your body to save you. According to the U.S. Coast Guard's 2023 recreational boating statistics, 85 percent of drowning victims were not wearing a life jacket. Federal law requires children under 13 to wear a PFD whenever a vessel is underway, except below deck or at anchor; state laws may go further. For adults, wearing it is voluntary but strongly recommended. Modern life jackets are slim, cooler, and less bulky than the old orange "horse collar" style — most look and feel like a fishing vest.

Pick one rated for what you do (paddling, fishing, offshore), make sure the fit is snug but not tight, and keep a few extras of correct sizes for guests. Boats over 16 feet must also carry one throwable Type IV flotation device — the ring or cushion you toss to someone in the water.

## File a float plan

A float plan is a short note you give to someone you trust before you launch. It tells them your boat's name, who is on board, when you left, where you are going (coordinates are best), and when you expect to be back. If you do not come back on time, that person knows when and where to start looking — and that one detail can make the difference between a rescue and a search that starts too late. Marinas, dockmasters, and many state agencies provide simple float plan forms; a text message to a family member works too.

## Communication

A handheld or fixed-mount VHF radio is the most reliable way to call for help on the water. VHF with Digital Selective Calling (DSC) and a registered MMSI number can send a distress call with your location at the press of a button. VHF channel 16 is the international distress and hailing channel — keep it monitored when underway.

Do not rely on a cell phone offshore. Cell coverage rarely extends more than a few miles from shore, and even close in, signal drops and battery deaths can leave you without a way to call out. For trips beyond easy VHF range, carry an EPIRB (Emergency Position-Indicating Radio Beacon) or a personal PLB — both signal your location to rescue services anywhere in the world.

## Check the weather and assess the trip

Look at the marine forecast on the morning of the trip, not the night before. Wind, wave height, and the trend over the next several hours matter more than the headline temperature. AnglerCast pulls live conditions on the Explore page so you can see what your destination looks like before you commit. If the conditions are at or beyond your boat's and your crew's capability, change the plan — pick a sheltered area, shorten the trip, or skip the day.

Before launch, do a quick risk check: Is the boat fueled and the engine running right? Are PFDs, fire extinguisher, flares, and signaling devices on board and in date? Is everyone dressed for the water temperature, not just the air temperature? A trip where any one answer is "no" is a trip worth delaying.

## The basics that should always be aboard

Beyond PFDs and the throwable, every recreational boat should carry visual distress signals appropriate to the waters you use (flares for coastal/offshore, an electric distress light for nighttime), a sound-signaling device (a horn or whistle), and a USCG-approved fire extinguisher if your boat has enclosed fuel or engine compartments. Carry a basic first-aid kit, drinking water, and a small toolkit. A whistle and signal mirror in the pocket of your PFD cost almost nothing and add real value if you end up in the water.

## Never boat under the influence

The U.S. Coast Guard's boating safety program lists boating under the influence (BUI) as one of the top contributors to recreational boating accidents. Sun, wind, and motion already slow your reactions; alcohol stacks on top of that. Designate a sober operator every trip. The legal limit for BUI matches drunk-driving limits in most states, and enforcement on the water is real.

## Get a free safety check

USCG Auxiliary and U.S. Power Squadrons offer free Vessel Safety Checks — a non-enforcement walkthrough where a trained examiner reviews your equipment against federal and state requirements. There is no penalty if anything is missing; you just get a list of what to add. It is one of the highest-value hours you can spend before the season starts.

# Why Month Matters

Fish are not evenly distributed across the calendar. Many species follow seasonal patterns — they migrate, spawn, or move between deep and shallow water at predictable times of year. Picking the right month for the species you want is one of the biggest decisions you can make before a trip.

## What the data shows

When AnglerCast pulls GBIF records for a species, each record carries an event date. Grouping those records by month reveals when this species has historically been seen in this area. Some species cluster heavily in two or three months and almost disappear the rest of the year. Others are spread evenly across the calendar.

For example, in the Mid-Atlantic, striped bass records concentrate in spring and fall when fish are migrating along the coast. Summer flounder records peak in summer when fish move inshore. Atlantic cod records concentrate in colder months when fish are closer to shore and shallower. Picking the wrong month for any of these species means fishing the area when the fish are historically somewhere else.

## How to use month in AnglerCast

On the Saltwater and Freshwater pages, you pick a species, a location, and a month. AnglerCast returns the sighting rate for that combination — the share of historical records that happened in your selected month. Try a few different months for the same species and location to see where the records cluster. If one month is dramatically higher than the others, that is a strong signal. If the records are evenly spread, the month matters less for that species in that area.

Always check the confidence flag. A high sighting rate in a low-confidence month means the pattern is based on very few records and may not reflect reality.

## Month is not the only signal

Month tells you when fish have been recorded in the past. It does not tell you whether today's conditions are right. A productive month with a bad weather front can be slower than a normally quiet month with ideal conditions. Use month to plan your trip and conditions to time the day.

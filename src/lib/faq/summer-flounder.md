# Summer Flounder (Paralichthys dentatus)

Also known as: fluke, northern fluke, flounder, hirame.

## What is a summer flounder?

The summer flounder, almost always called "fluke" by Northeast anglers, is a flatfish that lies on the bottom with both eyes on its upper (left) side. It is one of the most sought-after recreational and commercial fish on the Atlantic coast, found in inshore and offshore waters from Nova Scotia to Florida and most abundant from Cape Cod to Cape Hatteras. Fluke are strong, toothy ambush predators with excellent camouflage, and they can reach about 3 feet and over 20 pounds, living up to roughly 20 years. Females grow faster and larger than males, so the biggest "doormat" fluke are almost always female.

## What does a summer flounder eat?

Summer flounder are active ambush predators — unusually aggressive for a flatfish. They feed on small fish (such as sand eels, bay anchovies, silversides, and small menhaden), squid, shrimp, crabs, and other crustaceans. Rather than only lying in wait, fluke will also chase and pursue prey up off the bottom, which is why they readily hit moving baits and jigs.

## When is a summer flounder in season?

Fluke are an inshore summer presence in the Northeast — they move into bays, estuaries, and coastal waters as it warms and head offshore as it cools, which is the origin of the "summer" name. Recreational seasons, size limits, and bag limits are set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and individual states and change every year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does a summer flounder spawn?

Summer flounder spawn in the fall and winter, over the continental shelf, as the adults migrate from inshore summering grounds to deeper offshore wintering grounds (roughly 120–600 feet). The largest, oldest fish migrate and spawn first. Spawning runs from about September through December in the northern part of the range and as late as March farther south. The eggs are buoyant and drift in open water, most abundant between Cape Cod and Cape Hatteras in water around 57–63°F (14–17°C). Larvae drift toward shore, and juveniles settle into estuaries, which serve as important nursery grounds. Fluke typically mature at age 2 or 3.

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Saltwater. Summer flounder live in coastal ocean waters, bays, and estuaries. Juveniles depend on estuarine nursery habitat, but the species is marine and spawns offshore on the continental shelf.

## Fun fact / angler note

Big fluke earn the nickname "doormat" — dockside anglers compare the broad, flat body of a trophy fish to a doormat, and because females grow largest, nearly every doormat is a female. Fluke have a mouthful of sharp teeth, so anglers use care when unhooking them.

# Alewife (Alosa pseudoharengus)

Also known as: river herring (shared with blueback herring), sawbelly, gaspereau, branch herring.

## What is an alewife?

The alewife is a small, silvery, anadromous member of the herring family that, together with the very similar blueback herring, is collectively called "river herring." It lives most of its life in the ocean and returns to fresh water each spring to spawn, ranging along the Atlantic coast from Newfoundland to South Carolina and most abundant in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic. Alewives have a deeper body and larger eye than blueback herring, and the two are so alike they are managed together and often hard to tell apart. There are also landlocked alewife populations that live entirely in fresh water.

## What does an alewife eat?

Alewives are plankton feeders. They eat zooplankton — copepods and other small invertebrates — along with small crustaceans, fish eggs, and tiny fish. They feed in the water column in their schools, and in turn are a critical forage fish for striped bass, bluefish, cod, and many other predators and seabirds.

## When is an alewife in season?

Alewives are best known for their spring spawning runs, when huge numbers push up coastal streams and rivers. Many river herring runs are protected with strict limits or closures because populations have declined sharply; seasons and limits are set by state agencies and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission and change from year to year. Always check current local regulations before keeping fish.

## When does an alewife spawn?

Alewives are anadromous and ascend rivers, streams, lakes, and ponds to spawn in fresh water in spring, beginning earlier than blueback herring — in the Chesapeake region, alewife runs start in late February and early March. Adults migrate back downstream rapidly after spawning, and juveniles spend spring and early summer in tidal freshwater nursery areas before moving to sea. Alewives can spawn repeatedly over several years (roughly ages 3–8).

## Saltwater or freshwater?

Both. The alewife is anadromous: it spawns in fresh water (rivers, lakes, and ponds) but lives most of its life in the ocean. Some landlocked populations live entirely in fresh water.

## Fun fact / angler note

The spring alewife run is one of the great signs of spring in New England — fish ladders and stream mouths fill with herring, and the run feeds everything from ospreys to striped bass. Alewives are a classic bait for striped bass and a key forage species, which is why their conservation matters well beyond the fish itself.
